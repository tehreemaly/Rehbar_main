COMPREHENSIVE FARMING GUIDE
230 Essential Questions & Answers for Farmers Worldwide

NOTE: This guide covers all farmers globally — answers are applicable to most regions. Where region-specific advice is given, please consult your local agriculture extension officer for precise local recommendations. Emergency contacts for farmers worldwide are included at the end.

SECTION A: SEED & CROP SELECTION

1. Which wheat variety is best for my soil and climate?
Choose based on your region:
• Temperate/Cool regions (e.g., South Asia, Central Asia, Europe): Pak-13, Galaxy-13, Ariab-11, Johar-16; in Europe: Rialto, KWS Barrel; in USA: WB4458, Everest.
• Warm semi-arid regions (Middle East, N. Africa): ICARDA varieties like Sham-6, Cham-8.
• High rainfall zones: Choose lodging-resistant, disease-tolerant varieties.
• Always consult your national or provincial agriculture department for the latest approved varieties for your zone.

2. When is the best time to sow maize in my region?
• Tropical/subtropical (South Asia, Africa, Latin America): Sow at the onset of monsoon/rains (May–June for kharif).
• Temperate (USA, Europe): April–May when soil temperature reaches 10–13°C.
• Spring crop: After last frost, soil temp >10°C.
• Autumn/winter maize: Sub-tropical areas, Aug–Sep.
• General rule: Soil temperature at 5 cm depth should be 10–15°C minimum.

3. Which sugarcane variety gives the highest yield?
• South Asia (Pakistan/India): HSF-240, CPF-246, CPF-249, Thatta-10.
• Southeast Asia: KPS 01-25, Khonjee-3.
• Africa: N14, N19, Co 740.
• Australia: Q208, Q253.
• Americas: CP 88-1762 (USA), SP 81-3250 (Brazil).
• Select varieties suited to your soil type, rainfall, and growing season. Contact your local sugarcane research institute.

4. How do I choose quality seeds for my field?
• Buy certified seeds from registered dealers or government agencies.
• Check the seed tag for: variety name, lot number, germination %, purity %, moisture content.
• Germination should be ≥85% for most crops.
• Look for disease-free, uniform, plump seeds.
• Avoid discolored, shriveled, or damaged seeds.
• Do a germination test at home before bulk purchase.

5. What is the difference between hybrid and open-pollinated seed?
• Hybrid seeds: Cross between two parent lines. Give high, uniform yield. Seeds saved from hybrid crops do NOT perform the same next year — buy fresh each season.
• Open-pollinated (OP) seeds: Pollinated naturally. Seeds can be saved for next season. Lower but consistent yield.
• Hybrid advantages: Better vigor, uniformity, disease resistance, yield.
• OP advantages: Cheaper, seed-saving possible, better flavor (heirlooms).
• For commercial farming, hybrids are generally preferred for yield.

6. How do I test seed germination before sowing?
• Take 10–20 seeds randomly.
• Place between two moist paper towels or cloth.
• Keep at 20–25°C for 5–10 days (crop-dependent).
• Count germinated seeds.
• Germination %: (germinated seeds / total seeds) × 100.
• Acceptable: ≥80–85%. Below 70% — do not use or increase seed rate.
• For paddy/rice, use water float test: discard floating seeds.

7. What is the ideal seed rate for rice per acre?
• Direct seeding (wet): 8–10 kg/acre.
• Direct seeding (dry): 12–15 kg/acre.
• Transplanting: 4–5 kg nursery seed to transplant one acre (raise seedlings in 1/10th acre nursery).
• For hybrid rice: 2–3 kg/acre.
• Adjust based on seed size and germination rate.

8. How deep should I plant potato tubers?
• Plant tubers at 8–12 cm (3–5 inches) depth.
• In heavy/clay soils: 8 cm depth.
• In sandy/light soils: 10–12 cm depth.
• Row spacing: 60–75 cm; plant spacing: 20–30 cm.
• Deeper planting prevents greening and improves tuber quality.

9. Which tobacco varieties are recommended for cultivation?
• Virginia/Flue-cured: K-326, NC-95, Coker 176 (widely grown globally).
• Burley: TN 86, L8, DH14 (USA, Malawi, Brazil).
• Oriental: Samsun, Xanthi (Turkey, Greece, Bulgaria).
• Dark-fired: One Sucker, TN 86 (USA, Africa).
• Always verify which type your local buyer/processing factory requires before selecting a variety.

10. What crops can I grow after harvesting wheat?
• Summer/Kharif crops: Maize, rice, cotton, mung bean, sunflower, sorghum, fodder crops.
• Vegetables: Tomato, cucumber, okra, bitter gourd.
• Choose based on water availability and market demand.
• Allow 1–2 weeks between harvest and sowing for field preparation.
• Legumes (mung bean, cowpea) are excellent — they fix nitrogen and improve soil for the next crop.

11. Is it possible to grow sunflower in your region?
• Sunflower grows well in warm to hot climates, 18–35°C.
• Suitable for plains, semi-arid and sub-humid areas.
• Requires full sun, well-drained soil, 400–600 mm rainfall or irrigation.
• Varieties: Hysun-33, SF-187 (Pakistan), Helio-250 (Europe), Hyoleic 41 (Australia).
• Can be grown as spring or autumn crop depending on region.

12. What is the best sowing method for onion?
• Direct sowing: In rows, 1–2 cm deep, 15–20 cm row spacing.
• Transplanting (recommended for better yield): Raise nursery for 6–8 weeks, transplant at 15×10 cm spacing.
• Broadcasting: Not recommended (poor uniformity).
• For seed beds: Keep soil fine and moist.
• Best results with raised bed transplanting for commercial onion.

13. How far apart should tomato plants be spaced?
• Determinate varieties: 45–60 cm between plants, 75–90 cm between rows.
• Indeterminate (staked) varieties: 30–45 cm between plants, 90–120 cm between rows.
• For high tunnel/greenhouse: 40–50 cm between plants.
• Closer spacing reduces fruit size; wider spacing improves air circulation and reduces disease.

14. Can I grow garlic commercially?
• Yes — garlic grows well in cool climates, well-drained loamy soil, pH 6–7.
• Plant in autumn (temperate) or spring (colder areas) or Oct–Nov (South Asia).
• Planting depth: 3–5 cm, spacing: 10×15 cm.
• Yield: 4,000–8,000 kg/acre with good management.
• High-value crop for local and export markets.
• Varieties: Lasani, Desi (South Asia); Softneck (Spain, China); Hardneck (USA, Europe).

15. What is the best crop to grow in sandy soil?
• Sandy soils: Low water retention, low nutrients but good drainage.
• Best crops: Groundnut, sweet potato, watermelon, muskmelon, cowpea, sorghum, millet, sesame, carrot.
• Improve with compost/organic matter before planting high-demand crops.
• Mulching essential to conserve moisture.
• Avoid paddy/rice — needs clay-loam soil.

16. When should I plant cotton?
• Soil temperature must be consistently above 15°C (ideally 18–20°C).
• South Asia (Pakistan/India): April–May.
• USA (Belt): April–June depending on state.
• Africa: March–May.
• Planting too early in cold soil causes poor germination and disease.
• Use treated seeds in areas with soil-borne diseases.

17. How do I store seeds safely for the next season?
• Dry seeds to 10–12% moisture before storage.
• Store in airtight containers (glass jars, sealed tins, or sealed plastic bags with silica gel).
• Keep in cool, dry, dark place (10–15°C, <50% humidity).
• Label with variety, date, germination %.
• Mix with wood ash or neem powder for insect protection.
• Check every 2–3 months for moisture or pest damage.
• Hybrid seeds: Not recommended for saving — buy fresh.

18. What is the impact of late sowing on wheat yield?
• Each week of delay after optimum sowing date reduces yield by 1–3%.
• Late sowing shortens the vegetative period, reducing tillering and grain number.
• Crop faces heat stress at grain-filling stage.
• Increased disease risk (rusts, aphids) in spring.
• Mitigation: Use late-sowing tolerant varieties; increase seed rate by 10–15%.

19. How do I select disease-resistant seed varieties?
• Check variety description sheets from national seed certification bodies.
• Look for ratings: Resistant (R), Moderately Resistant (MR), Susceptible (S).
• Consult local agricultural extension officers or universities.
• Prioritize resistance to common diseases in your area (e.g., wheat rust, blight, wilt).
• Disease resistance alone is not enough — also consider yield potential and climate fit.

20. Which vegetable crops are most profitable?
• High-value crops globally: Tomato, capsicum/pepper, cucumber, garlic, onion, peas, green beans, broccoli.
• Off-season vegetables fetch higher prices — plan accordingly.
• Crops near urban markets give better returns.
• Check local market demand before deciding.
• Protected cultivation (tunnels, greenhouses) enables year-round high-value production.

SECTION B: SOIL PREPARATION & MANAGEMENT

1. How do I know if my soil is acidic or alkaline?
• Get a soil test from a laboratory (most accurate).
• Use a pH meter or soil pH test kit (available at agri stores).
• pH <7 = acidic; pH 7 = neutral; pH >7 = alkaline/basic.
• Indicators: Acidic soils often have moss, low yields, aluminum toxicity. Alkaline soils show yellowing (iron/zinc deficiency), white crusty surface.
• Most crops prefer pH 6–7.5.

2. What type of soil is best for wheat cultivation?
• Loamy or clay-loam soil is ideal: Good water retention and drainage.
• pH: 6.0–7.5.
• Well-drained, fertile soil with good organic matter (>1%).
• Sandy soils: Possible with irrigation management and added organic matter.
• Heavy clay: Poor drainage — needs raised beds or tiling.

3. How many times should I plough before sowing?
• First ploughing: Deep (20–25 cm) — breaks compact layers.
• Second ploughing: Cross-direction for thorough soil breakup.
• 2–3 harrowings: To bring soil to fine tilth.
• Total: 1 deep plough + 2–3 cultivations is standard.
• For vegetable seedbeds: More passes needed for fine, clod-free soil.
• Avoid over-ploughing — it destroys soil structure.

4. What is deep ploughing and when should I do it?
• Deep ploughing: Tilling to 35–60 cm depth (vs. normal 15–20 cm).
• Breaks subsoil hardpan, improves drainage, buries surface weeds/disease.
• Do every 3–5 years, not annually (damages soil structure if done too often).
• Best time: After harvest in summer, before monsoon rains soften soil.
• Use subsoiler or deep ripper implement.

5. How do I improve sandy soil for better crop growth?
• Add organic matter: 10–15 tonnes compost/FYM per acre annually.
• Apply clay subsoil (if available) to improve texture.
• Use mulching (straw, plastic) to retain moisture.
• Grow green manure crops (dhaincha, cowpea) and incorporate.
• Reduce leaching by split-applying fertilizers.
• Drip/trickle irrigation is most efficient for sandy soils.

6. My soil is very hard after harvest — how do I fix it?
• This is soil compaction — common after heavy machinery or rain.
• Chisel plough or subsoil while soil has some moisture.
• Apply organic matter (compost/manure) — microbes and earthworms loosen soil.
• Avoid working soil when wet.
• Plant deep-rooted cover crops (radish, sunflower) — roots break hardpan.
• Reduce heavy machinery traffic; use controlled traffic lanes.

7. How do I reduce soil salinity in my field?
• Apply gypsum (calcium sulphate): 200–400 kg/acre, depending on soil test.
• Leach salts: Apply heavy irrigation and drain water out.
• Grow salt-tolerant crops: Barley, sugar beet, cotton, Kallar grass, sesbania.
• Improve drainage with field drains or tile drains.
• Apply organic matter to help flush salts and improve structure.
• Avoid over-irrigation with saline water.

8. What is the recommended time for soil testing?
• Best time: After harvest, before the next crop.
• Allow 2–3 months before sowing so results guide fertilizer decisions.
• Test every 2–3 years minimum; annually for intensive farming.
• Avoid testing immediately after applying fertilizers (wait 6–8 weeks).

9. Where can I get my soil tested?
• Government soil testing labs: Agriculture departments in each province/state.
• Universities: Agriculture faculties often offer soil testing services.
• Private labs: Search for certified soil testing labs in your district.
• Online: NARC (Pakistan), ICAR (India), USDA/Extension (USA), DEFRA labs (UK).
• Mobile soil testing vans: Available in many countries during sowing seasons.
• Emergency contacts: Your local agriculture extension officer (see contacts section below).

10. How much organic matter does my soil need?
• Ideal organic matter: 2–3% for most crops.
• Most South Asian soils: <1% — need building up.
• To increase by 0.1%: Apply 2–3 tonnes compost/manure per acre per year.
• Add crop residues, green manures, and compost regularly.
• Test organic matter every 3–5 years.

11. What is subsoil hardpan and how do I break it?
• Hardpan: A dense, impermeable layer 25–50 cm below surface — restricts roots and drainage.
• Causes: Repeated ploughing at the same depth, heavy machinery, natural clay accumulation.
• Break with: Subsoiler, ripper, or chisel plough (penetrates 40–60 cm).
• Do when soil is dry enough for the tool to shatter, not smear.
• One-time deep tillage can last 5–10 years if traffic is managed.

12. Should I add lime to my soil?
• Add lime if pH <5.5 (acid soils).
• Agricultural lime (calcium carbonate): 200–1000 kg/acre depending on soil test.
• Dolomitic lime: Use if magnesium is also low.
• Effects last 3–5 years.
• Do not apply lime and phosphate fertilizer at the same time.
• Most South Asian and tropical soils are alkaline — do NOT add lime; instead use gypsum or sulfur.

13. How does crop rotation improve soil health?
• Breaks pest and disease cycles (no host buildup).
• Legumes (pulses) fix atmospheric nitrogen — reduces fertilizer need.
• Different root depths improve soil structure at multiple levels.
• Reduces weed pressure from crop-specific weeds.
• Example: Wheat → Mung bean → Maize → Vegetables.
• Minimum: Rotate at least 3 crops over 3 years.

14. What cover crops help prevent soil erosion in hilly areas?
• Vetiver grass: Deep roots, excellent erosion control on slopes.
• Cowpea, soybean: Summer cover, nitrogen-fixing.
• Oats, rye, barley: Winter cover crops.
• Berseem (Egyptian clover), alfalfa: Good for slopes in South Asia/Middle East.
• Sesbania, dhaincha: Tropical areas.
• Plant along contour lines on slopes for best effect.

15. How deep should I till before planting potatoes?
• Minimum 30–35 cm depth.
• Potatoes need deep, loose, well-aerated soil for tuber expansion.
• Use ripper/subsoiler if hardpan exists below 25 cm.
• Make raised ridges 30–35 cm high for best results.
• Fine tilth essential — large clods cause misshapen tubers.

16. What is the best tillage method for water-logged fields?
• Install surface drains and field ditches first.
• Mole drainage: Pulls a torpedo-shaped implement underground to create drainage channels.
• Raised bed planting: Keep roots above waterline.
• Do not cultivate when soil is waterlogged — causes severe compaction.
• Summer deep ploughing after drainage helps break up anaerobic layers.

17. How do I prepare raised beds for vegetables?
• Till to 20–25 cm, apply compost/FYM.
• Make beds 90–120 cm wide, 15–20 cm high.
• Leave 30–40 cm channels between beds for irrigation and walking.
• Level the surface with a rake.
• Plant on top and sides of bed.
• Raised beds improve drainage, aeration, and root development.

18. What causes soil compaction and how can I prevent it?
• Causes: Heavy machinery, tilling wet soil, lack of organic matter, repeated shallow tillage.
• Prevention:
  - Avoid field operations when soil is wet.
  - Controlled traffic farming — confine machinery to fixed lanes.
  - Add organic matter annually.
  - Vary tillage depth each season.
  - Use cover crops with strong roots.

19. How do I reclaim waterlogged land for farming?
• Install surface field drains (open channels) to remove standing water.
• Install subsurface tile drains if budget allows.
• Apply gypsum for sodic/saline soils.
• Grow salt/water-tolerant crops initially: Kallar grass, sesbania, rice.
• Add organic matter to improve soil structure.
• Takes 2–5 years of management for full reclamation.

20. Is no-till farming suitable for most regions?
• No-till (zero tillage): Sow directly without ploughing.
• Advantages: Less fuel/cost, preserves soil structure, reduces erosion.
• Works best: In well-drained soils with established crop residue cover.
• Challenges: Higher weed pressure (herbicide needed), disease buildup if no rotation, poor seedbed in heavy soils.
• Recommended: Pair with crop rotation and residue management.
• In South Asia: Zero-till wheat after rice has shown 30–40% cost saving.

SECTION C: IRRIGATION MANAGEMENT

1. How often should I irrigate wheat in winter?
• In general: 4–6 irrigations per season depending on rainfall and soil type.
• Critical stages: Crown root initiation (21 DAS), tillering (45 DAS), jointing (65 DAS), flowering (85 DAS), grain filling (100 DAS).
• Sandy soils: Irrigate more frequently (every 10–12 days).
• Clay-loam soils: Every 15–20 days.
• Reduce or skip if rainfall exceeds 25 mm in a week.

2. What is the critical irrigation stage for maize?
• Most critical: Tasseling and silking (6–8 weeks after sowing) — water stress here causes 25–50% yield loss.
• Also important: Knee-high stage (30–35 DAS) and grain filling (dough stage).
• Do not let soil dry out between sowing and V6 stage (6-leaf stage).

3. How do I measure soil moisture before irrigating?
• Feel method: Take soil from 15 cm depth. If it forms a ball but falls apart — fine. If dusty — irrigate. If muddy — wait.
• Tensiometer: Insert into soil — reads suction; irrigate at >60 kPa for most crops.
• Soil moisture meter: Digital probe sensors — quick and accurate.
• Gravimetric method: Weigh soil before and after oven drying — most accurate but lab-based.

4. Is drip irrigation suitable for vegetable farms?
• Yes — drip irrigation is highly suitable and recommended for vegetables.
• Saves 30–50% water vs. flood irrigation.
• Reduces disease (foliage stays dry), reduces weed growth.
• Can be combined with fertigation (fertilizer through drip).
• Higher initial cost but quickly recovered through water and fertilizer savings.
• Suitable for: Tomato, pepper, cucumber, onion, garlic, potato.

5. What is the water requirement per acre for rice?
• Total water requirement: 900–1200 mm per season (approx. 36–48 acre-inches).
• Equivalent to about 600,000–800,000 liters/acre.
• Includes: Land soaking, nursery, transplanting, crop period.
• Water-saving methods: Alternate wetting and drying (AWD) can save 15–30% water.
• Direct seeded rice uses 20–30% less water than transplanted.

6. How do I prevent waterlogging in my field?
• Install surface drains around field perimeter.
• Build field channels at 1–2% slope for gravity drainage.
• Use raised bed planting.
• Avoid over-irrigation — match water to crop demand.
• Improve soil drainage with deep tillage and organic matter.
• Subsurface tile drains for severe cases.

7. What is the best irrigation method for sloped fields?
• Contour furrow irrigation: Furrows run along the slope contour — most practical for moderate slopes.
• Drip irrigation: Best for steep slopes — no runoff.
• Sprinkler with low application rate: For gentle slopes.
• Border strip irrigation: Only for very gentle slopes (<0.5%).
• Avoid flood/basin irrigation on slopes — severe erosion risk.

8. How do I check if I am over-irrigating my crop?
• Signs: Waterlogged areas, yellowing leaves (oxygen deficiency), stunted growth, fungal diseases, standing water 24 hours after irrigation.
• Check soil: If water comes out when squeezed — over-irrigated.
• Use a tensiometer: If reading is very low (<10 kPa) before irrigating — you are irrigating too soon.
• Track: Keep an irrigation diary to know when and how much you irrigated.

9. Can I use sprinkler irrigation for wheat?
• Yes — sprinkler irrigation works well for wheat.
• Allows uniform water distribution, especially on uneven land.
• Reduce disease risk: Do not irrigate in the evening; irrigate early morning.
• Avoid sprinklers at flowering stage (may damage pollination in some crops).
• More costly than surface irrigation but more efficient.

10. What is furrow irrigation and how does it work?
• Furrow irrigation: Water flows in small channels (furrows) between crop rows.
• Water infiltrates laterally into the root zone.
• Suitable for: Row crops (maize, cotton, potato, sugarcane).
• Furrow length: 50–200 m depending on soil and slope.
• Efficient when furrows are properly graded (0.1–0.5% slope).
• Avoid: Very long furrows on flat ground — water doesn't reach the end.

11. How do I reduce water wastage during flood irrigation?
• Level land precisely — laser leveling saves 20–30% water.
• Reduce irrigation time — don't let water stand for more than 2–3 hours.
• Use shorter irrigation sets and alternate furrow irrigation.
• Apply mulch to reduce evaporation.
• Irrigate at night or early morning to reduce evaporation losses.
• Reuse tail water by installing a collection pond.

12. What happens if I miss irrigation at the flowering stage?
• Severe yield loss: 20–60% depending on crop and duration of stress.
• Wheat: Affects grain number and weight.
• Maize: Causes poor pollination, missing kernels.
• Rice: Reduces panicle filling and grain weight.
• Vegetables: Cause flower/fruit drop, blossom end rot (tomato).
• If missed, apply irrigation as soon as possible; damage is often irreversible.

13. How do I irrigate during a drought period?
• Prioritize critical growth stages (see crop-specific critical stages above).
• Apply deficit irrigation — give 50–70% of normal water at less critical stages.
• Mulch heavily to conserve soil moisture.
• Apply anti-transpirants (kaolin, silicon) to reduce water loss through leaves.
• Grow drought-tolerant varieties.
• Harvest rainwater from rooftops/roads into farm ponds.

14. What is the role of mulching in saving irrigation water?
• Mulch reduces evaporation from soil surface by 30–70%.
• Materials: Straw, plastic film, crop residues, dry leaves.
• Black plastic mulch: Best for vegetables — warms soil, prevents weeds.
• Organic mulch: Improves soil as it decomposes.
• Apply 5–10 cm thick around plants.
• Especially valuable in hot, dry conditions.

15. How many irrigations does sugarcane need in total?
• Total: 20–30 irrigations per season (12–14 months).
• Heavy irrigation needed: Germination, tillering, grand growth period.
• Critical stage: Grand growth (4–9 months after planting) — do not stress.
• Reduce water 2 months before harvest (improves sugar content).
• Water requirement: 1,500–2,500 mm per season.

16. What is the best time of day to irrigate crops?
• Early morning (5–9 AM): Best — lower evaporation, reduces disease risk.
• Evening: Acceptable but increases fungal disease risk (wet foliage overnight).
• Afternoon/midday: Worst — highest evaporation losses.
• For drip/subsurface: Time is less critical as water goes directly to roots.

17. How do I manage irrigation in saline water conditions?
• Irrigate more frequently with smaller amounts to leach salts downward.
• Apply 10–20% extra water as a leaching fraction.
• Drip irrigation minimizes salt accumulation near roots.
• Grow salt-tolerant crops: Barley, cotton, sugar beet, date palm.
• Mix saline water with fresh water if possible.
• Monitor soil EC regularly; if >4 dS/m, take corrective action.

18. What is deficit irrigation and when should I use it?
• Deficit irrigation: Deliberately applying less water than full crop requirement.
• Use when: Water is scarce, irrigation costs are high, or drought conditions.
• Apply full water at critical stages; reduce at vegetative and ripening stages.
• Can save 20–40% water with only 5–15% yield reduction.
• Example: Skip 1–2 irrigations during early vegetative stage in wheat.

19. How can I measure the water flow in my field channel?
• Float method: Place float in channel, measure time to travel a known distance; multiply by cross-section area.
• Current meter: Measures flow velocity directly.
• Weir/flume: Install a V-notch or rectangular weir — measure water height, use formula.
• Portable ultrasonic flow meter: Available for rental from irrigation departments.
• Simple bucket method: For small pipes/channels.

20. Is bore well water safe for all crops?
• Not always — test bore water annually for EC (salinity), pH, SAR (sodium), and heavy metals.
• EC <0.7 dS/m: Suitable for all crops.
• EC 0.7–3.0 dS/m: Use salt-tolerant crops with leaching.
• EC >3.0 dS/m: Harmful to most crops; mix with fresh water.
• High iron/manganese: Damages drip emitters and affects crop nutrition.
• Contact your water testing lab or irrigation department for analysis.

SECTION D: WEATHER & CLIMATE MANAGEMENT

1. How does late monsoon affect kharif crop planting?
• Delayed monsoon shortens the growing season for kharif crops.
• Short-duration varieties become necessary.
• Sow at first opportunity — even limited early moisture is enough to germinate.
• Delay reduces yield in rice, maize, cotton.
• Have irrigation backup ready if monsoon fails.

2. What should I do if rain is delayed during wheat sowing?
• Do not wait too long — sow with stored soil moisture or light irrigation.
• Apply pre-sowing irrigation (pali) to moisten the seedbed.
• Use drought-tolerant early-maturing varieties.
• Reduce seed rate slightly on dry seedbeds.
• If delay is >3 weeks past optimum, switch to a late-sowing variety.

3. How do I protect my crops from unexpected frost?
• Short-term: Irrigate before a frost event — wet soil holds more heat.
• Apply light irrigation during frost night — latent heat released protects to -2°C.
• Use frost covers/row covers for vegetables.
• Windbreaks reduce frost damage in orchards.
• Smoke generation (smudge pots) in orchards.
• Plant frost-sensitive crops away from frost hollows (low-lying areas).

4. What crops can I grow with only rainfall and no irrigation?
• Rainfed suitable crops: Wheat (300+ mm/season), sorghum, millet, chickpea, lentil, groundnut, sesame, sunflower, maize (600+ mm/season).
• Drought-tolerant crops: Sorghum, millet (250 mm), chickpea.
• Match crop water requirement to local annual/seasonal rainfall.
• Use conservation agriculture and mulching to maximize rain use.

5. How does hailstorm damage affect wheat yield and what can I do?
• At vegetative stage: Significant damage but crop can recover if growing point is intact.
• At grain filling: Very serious — knocked grains cannot recover.
• After hail: Apply foliar fertilizer (urea 2%) and fungicide to prevent disease entry.
• Check if tillers and main stem growing point is alive.
• Report to agriculture/insurance department immediately for assessment.
• Anti-hail nets in orchards can protect high-value crops.

6. When does the monsoon typically start in South Asia?
• Pakistan/India northwest: July–August.
• India/Bangladesh (eastern): June.
• Sri Lanka/southeast India: October–December (northeast monsoon).
• Check Pakistan Meteorological Department / India IMD for annual forecasts.
• El Niño years often delay monsoon — follow forecasts for early planning.

7. How can I protect vegetables from heavy rain damage?
• Use raised beds for better drainage.
• Cover crops with plastic tunnels or row covers.
• Stake and tie plants before rain season.
• Apply fungicide after rain (preventive).
• Ensure field has drainage channels so water does not stand.
• Avoid sprinkler/overhead irrigation before rain events.

8. What is the minimum rainfall needed for rainfed wheat?
• Minimum: 250–300 mm well-distributed from sowing to heading.
• Ideal: 350–450 mm.
• Critical rain periods: At sowing (germination), tillering, and grain filling.
• Below 250 mm: Use drought-resistant varieties and conservation practices.

9. How do I drain excess water from fields after heavy rain?
• Open field bunds (earthen barriers) on the lower side to release water.
• Dig emergency drainage cuts to the nearest natural drain.
• Use portable pumps if gravity drainage is not available.
• After water drains, avoid entering field until soil is firm.
• Repair field drains before next rain event.

10. What crops are suitable for dry/arid regions?
• Sorghum, millet, cowpea, moth bean: Survive on 200–350 mm rain.
• Groundnut, sesame, chickpea: 300–450 mm.
• Date palm: Extremely drought-tolerant.
• Watermelon, muskmelon: Sandy, hot, dry conditions.
• Kinnow/citrus, olive: Mediterranean-type dry conditions.
• Supplement with drip/trickle irrigation for higher returns.

11. Does fog in winter affect wheat disease levels?
• Yes — prolonged fog keeps leaf surfaces wet, encouraging yellow rust, powdery mildew, and Septoria leaf blotch.
• High humidity above 85% for >10 hours/day is a major disease driver.
• Monitor crops closely after foggy periods.
• Apply preventive fungicide if conditions persist more than 3 days.

12. How do I estimate rainwater harvesting for my farm?
• Harvestable water = Catchment area (m²) × Rainfall depth (m) × Runoff coefficient.
• Runoff coefficient: 0.6–0.8 for bare soil; 0.3–0.5 for vegetated land.
• Example: 1 hectare, 50 mm rain, coefficient 0.5 = 1,000 × 0.05 × 0.5 = 250 m³ = 250,000 liters.
• Build farm ponds, earthen tanks (kunds) to store runoff water.

13. How do I protect fruit orchards from cold waves?
• Apply irrigation before cold event (heat buffer in soil).
• Apply straw or plastic mulch around tree bases.
• Whitewash tree trunks to prevent bark splitting.
• Use windbreaks (tree rows) on north/northwest side.
• Anti-frost sprinklers (run during frost night — latent heat protection).
• Remove low-growing vegetation under trees — cold sinks into depressions.

14. What causes sudden crop lodging after strong winds?
• Weak stem base from excess nitrogen (tall, thin stems).
• Shallow root system from compacted soil.
• Heavy grain load at top (late-stage).
• Prevention: Use lodging-resistant varieties, balanced fertilizers, avoid excess nitrogen.
• Apply growth regulators (e.g., trinexapac-ethyl for wheat) to shorten internodes.
• Lodged crop: Harvest quickly to avoid mold and yield loss.

15. Does high humidity increase fungal diseases in crops?
• Yes — humidity >80% and temperatures of 15–25°C are ideal for most fungal pathogens.
• Most affected: Wheat rust, late blight (potato), downy mildew, botrytis.
• Management: Improve air circulation, avoid dense planting, schedule irrigation in morning, apply preventive fungicide.

16. How do I prepare my field for rabi sowing after heavy kharif rain?
• Wait for soil to reach "field capacity" then dry slightly — test: no mud sticking to shoes.
• Apply light tillage (cultivator) to break surface crust.
• Re-level if water has eroded bed structure.
• Apply basal fertilizer.
• Sow promptly — delay losses accrue daily.

17. What weather conditions lead to a locust attack?
• Locust outbreaks follow: Heavy rains in desert areas (breeding) + warm, dry winds (migration).
• Desert Locust (Schistocerca gregaria): Breeds in areas that received unusually high rainfall.
• Early warning: FAO Desert Locust Information Service sends alerts.
• Monitor: Unusual presence of hoppers or swarms in border areas.
• Report immediately to plant protection department.

18. Is there a crop insurance scheme for rain damage?
• Pakistan: Fasal Bima (crop insurance) pilot programs through provincial governments.
• India: Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana (PMFBY) — subsidized crop insurance.
• USA: Federal Crop Insurance Program (USDA RMA).
• Europe: Various EU-supported agri-insurance schemes.
• Contact your agriculture department or bank for enrollment details.

19. How does temperature change affect wheat grain filling?
• Optimal grain filling temperature: 15–20°C.
• Each degree above 30°C reduces grain weight by 3–5%.
• Late sowing exposes grain filling to high spring temperatures — avoid.
• High temperature at flowering: Poor fertilization, reduced grain number.
• Select heat-tolerant varieties where late spring heat is a risk.

20. Where can I get reliable weather forecasts for farming?
• Pakistan: Pakistan Meteorological Department (PMD) — pmd.gov.pk; Agri Weather App.
• India: India Meteorological Department — imd.gov.in; Meghdoot app.
• Global: Weather.com, AccuWeather, Windy.com.
• FAO: GIEWS for crop/weather monitoring.
• Apps: Farmlogs, Climate FieldView (include forecast and agronomic alerts).
• Local: Krishi Vigyan Kendras (India), agriculture extension offices.

SECTION E: CANAL WATER & IRRIGATION SYSTEMS

1. How do I register my field for canal water turns (wari)?
• Visit your local Sub-Divisional Officer (SDO) of Irrigation Department with your land ownership documents.
• Provide field number (khasra), land area, village name.
• In Pakistan: Register through Irrigation Department offices; in India: through Water User Associations or Block offices.
• Annual registration usually required before each irrigation season.

2. What is the canal water schedule (wadda) for my area?
• Canal water schedules (warabandi) are fixed rosters allocating specific days and times.
• Obtain from: Irrigation Patwari (canal overseer), SDO office, or district irrigation office.
• Online: Some provinces publish schedules online (search your provincial Irrigation Department website).
• Each farmer receives water in proportion to land area registered.

3. How do I file a complaint if my canal water supply is stopped?
• First step: Contact local Canal Overseer / Amin or Lambardaar.
• Next: Visit Sub-Divisional Officer (SDO) Irrigation.
• Formal complaint: Executive Engineer (XEN) Irrigation Division.
• In Pakistan: You can also contact the Irrigation Department helpline or submit a complaint at the district office.
• Keep records: Dates, times, crop affected, witnesses.

4. What is the role of the Irrigation Department?
• Plans and maintains main canals, distributaries, and minors.
• Allocates water using warabandi system.
• Resolves water disputes between farmers.
• Issues canal water connections.
• Maintains canal structures (regulators, gates, bridges).
• Oversees lining and rehabilitation programs.

5. How is canal water distributed fairly between farmers?
• Warabandi system: Time-based rotation — each farmer receives water for a fixed duration proportional to their land.
• Duty: Volume of water per unit area of land.
• Distribution from head to tail should be supervised to prevent head-reach farmers taking excess water.
• Water User Associations can help enforce fair distribution at local level.

6. What is a watercourse and how is it maintained?
• A watercourse is a small farmer-made/managed channel that carries canal water from the distribution point (mogha/outlet) to individual fields.
• Maintenance: Farmers in the watercourse group (chak) collectively:
  - De-silt and clean the channel before each season.
  - Repair breaches.
  - Control water theft.
• Lined watercourses reduce losses by 30–40%.

7. Who is responsible for cleaning the minor canal near my field?
• Main canals and branches: Irrigation Department.
• Distributaries and minors: Irrigation Department, but farmer associations assist.
• Watercourses (farm channels): Farmers collectively responsible.
• If minor canal is blocked: Report to Irrigation Sub-Division office.

8. How do I measure how much water I received from the canal?
• Flow rate × time = volume.
• Use weir formula: Q = 1.84 × L × H^1.5 (for rectangular weir; L = crest length, H = head).
• Simpler: Measure mogha opening size — standard sizes are calibrated; multiply by time.
• Keep a water receipt log at your outlet.

9. Can I dig a farm pond to store canal water?
• Generally allowed with permission from Irrigation Department.
• Helps store water during high-flow periods for use during low-flow or closure.
• Line pond with plastic or compacted clay to reduce seepage.
• Volume: Plan for enough to irrigate critical crop stages during closure.
• Check local water rights laws — storage rights may be separate from flow rights.

10. What is a distributary canal?
• Canal system hierarchy: Main canal → Branch canal → Distributary canal → Minor canal → Watercourse → Field.
• Distributary: Offtakes from a branch canal, serves 1,000–4,000 hectares.
• Branch canal: Offtakes from main canal, serves large command areas.
• Distributary is closer to farm level; branch is larger and further upstream.

11. What happens if a farmer blocks a canal illegally?
• Under Canal and Drainage Act (Pakistan/India): Illegal interference with a canal is a criminal offense.
• Penalties: Fines (PKR 5,000–50,000 in Pakistan), imprisonment up to 1 year.
• Irrigation Department can forcibly remove obstruction.
• Report to SDO or Executive Engineer immediately.

12. How do I apply for a new canal water connection?
• Prepare: Land ownership documents, khasra number, sketch map.
• Submit application to Divisional Canal Officer or SDO (Irrigation).
• Technical feasibility is assessed.
• Pay prescribed fees.
• Connection (outlet/mogha) is installed by Irrigation Department.
• Process time: 1–6 months depending on area and season.

13. What is the canal command area?
• Command area: Total agricultural land that can theoretically be irrigated from a canal.
• Your field is within the command area if it is below the canal level and can receive gravity flow.
• Fields above the canal level are outside command — need lift irrigation.

14. Is there a warabandi system and how does it work?
• Warabandi: Rotational water distribution system used in Pakistan, India (Punjab, Haryana, Rajasthan).
• Each farmer in a watercourse gets water for a fixed time slot proportional to their land.
• Time turn = (Farmer's area / Total watercourse area) × Rotation period (usually 7 days).
• Applies from mogha downward through the watercourse.
• Managed jointly by farmers and Irrigation Department.

15. How do I protect my watercourse from seepage losses?
• Concrete or brick lining: Most effective — reduces seepage by 70–80%.
• Compacted clay lining: Cheaper — reduces seepage by 30–50%.
• Plastic film lining: Low cost, short life.
• Clean and de-silt regularly — sediment buildup causes breaches.
• Fill rat holes and damaged banks promptly.

16. What government schemes exist for lining watercourses?
• Pakistan: OFWM (On-Farm Water Management) — subsidized lining of watercourses (50–75% government cost-sharing).
• India: CADWM (Command Area Development and Water Management) program.
• Apply through your local Agriculture/Irrigation Department office.
• Farmer contribution: Usually 25–50% (labour or cash).

17. How do I resolve a water dispute with a neighboring farmer?
• First: Discuss directly — most disputes are resolved this way.
• Village level: Involve village headman (lambardaar) or elder.
• Official: File written complaint with Irrigation Patwari or SDO.
• Legal: Canal courts (Revenue/Irrigation courts) under Canal Act.
• Keep records: Written, dated statements of your water turns and observed violations.

18. What is the penalty for stealing canal water?
• Pakistan Canal and Drainage Act 1873: Fine up to PKR 50,000 and/or imprisonment up to 1 year for first offense.
• India Canal Acts (state-specific): Similar fines and imprisonment.
• Irrigation Department can remove obstruction and seal outlet.
• Repeat offenses carry higher penalties.

19. Can I use canal water for drip irrigation systems?
• Yes — but canal water must be filtered before use in drip systems.
• Canal water often contains silt, algae, and debris that clog emitters.
• Install: Primary filter (screen/mesh), sand filter, and disk filter in sequence.
• Flush drip laterals every 2–3 irrigations.
• Check local regulations — some canal authorities restrict non-flood water use.

20. What are the canal closure months and how do I plan irrigation?
• Pakistan Punjab/KPK canals: Usually closed for maintenance in January (Rabi) and October (Kharif) for 4–8 weeks.
• Plan: Store water in farm ponds before closure.
• Use bore well/tubewell as backup during closure.
• Schedule high water-demand crop stages to avoid closure period.
• Check exact closure dates from your local Canal Circle office annually.

SECTION F: PEST MANAGEMENT

1. How do I identify army worm in my maize crop?
• Fall Armyworm (Spodoptera frugiperda): Look for irregular holes/ragged feeding on young leaves; frass (excrement) in whorl.
• African Armyworm (Spodoptera exempta): Mass movement of caterpillars across fields.
• Larvae: Green/brown/gray with Y-shaped marks on head capsule (FAW).
• Damage is fastest in young plants (V1–V6 stage).
• Scout whorls of 20 plants per acre; take action if >20% infested.

2. What is the best way to control termites in a newly sown field?
• Seed treatment: Imidacloprid 600 FS @ 2–4 ml/kg seed.
• Soil treatment: Chlorpyrifos 40 EC @ 2 L/acre in irrigation water before sowing.
• Apply FYM/compost that is well-rotted — raw manure attracts termites.
• Remove old crop stumps and wood debris from field.
• Deep ploughing exposes termite colonies to sun and predators.

3. How do I protect my wheat from aphid attack in spring?
• Monitor weekly from January onwards.
• Economic Threshold Level (ETL): 50–100 aphids/tiller before taking action.
• Natural enemies: Ladybugs, lacewings, parasitic wasps — encourage by avoiding broad-spectrum pesticides.
• Spray: Imidacloprid, acetamiprid, or dimethoate if ETL is exceeded.
• One timely spray at ETL saves yield loss without overuse of pesticides.

4. What is the economic threshold level (ETL) for pest control?
• ETL: The pest population level at which the cost of control equals the value of crop saved.
• Below ETL: Natural enemies and crop tolerance keep losses acceptable — do not spray.
• Above ETL: Spray to prevent economic loss.
• Examples: Wheat aphid: 50–100 per tiller; Rice stem borer: 5–10% dead hearts; Cotton whitefly: 15 per leaf.
• Always scout before deciding to spray — ETL varies by crop, pest, and season.

5. How do I control whitefly in tomatoes?
• Yellow sticky traps: Monitor and mass-trap adults.
• Reflective mulch: Deters landing.
• Neem-based spray (azadirachtin): Effective against nymphs.
• Insecticide rotation: Spiromesifen → Imidacloprid → Pyriproxyfen (rotate to prevent resistance).
• Avoid dense planting; ensure good air circulation.
• Remove and destroy heavily infested leaves.

6. What are the main pests of cotton and how to control them?
• Whitefly (Bemisia tabaci): Yellow sticky traps, neem, spiromesifen, imidacloprid.
• Pink bollworm (Pectinophora gossypiella): Pheromone traps, spinosad, emamectin benzoate.
• American bollworm: Bt spray, spinosad, cypermethrin (if ETL exceeded).
• Jassid/leafhopper: Imidacloprid seed treatment.
• Thrips: Abamectin, spinosad.
• Use IPM: Pheromone traps + biological control + chemical only at ETL.

7. How do I identify pink bollworm damage in cotton?
• Entry holes in squares (flower buds) with frass.
• "Rosetted" flowers: Petals stuck together (larva feeds inside).
• Damaged bolls: Internal feeding, sticky lint with frass.
• Larvae: Pink/white caterpillars inside bolls at 3rd–4th instar.
• Use pheromone traps: Capture >8 moths/trap/week = spray threshold reached.

8. How do I control fruit fly in mango orchards?
• Protein bait spray: GF-120 NF (spinosad + bait) applied to canopy weekly.
• Methyl eugenol traps: Hang 4–6 traps/acre from fruit development.
• Cover individual fruits with paper/muslin bags.
• Collect and destroy fallen infested fruits daily.
• Field sanitation: Remove all dropped fruit.
• Chemical: Malathion bait spray (only if traps show high populations).

9. What are natural enemies of aphids I can encourage?
• Ladybird beetles (Coccinella spp.): Adults and larvae consume 100+ aphids/day.
• Lacewings (Chrysoperla spp.): Larvae are voracious aphid predators.
• Parasitic wasps (Aphidius spp.): Lay eggs inside aphids.
• Syrphid flies: Larvae feed on aphids.
• Encourage by: Planting flowers (marigold, mustard) around field, avoiding broad-spectrum pesticides, leaving field margins unsprayed.

10. How do I scout my field properly for pest populations?
• Use W-shaped or zigzag pattern across the field.
• Check at least 20–30 plants per acre (more for small fields).
• Record: Pest count per plant/leaf/stem, plant stage, % infested.
• Check crop from multiple levels: Canopy, mid-plant, base, soil.
• Scout weekly during crop growth; more frequently during high-risk periods.
• Use field scouting apps (CABI, FAO) on your smartphone.

11. What causes stem borer damage in rice and how to control it?
• Yellow stem borer (Scirpophaga incertulas): Larvae enter stem → "dead hearts" at vegetative, "white ears" at reproductive stage.
• Management:
  - Resistant varieties (IR36, IR64 tolerant).
  - Remove and destroy egg masses on seedlings.
  - Pheromone traps for monitoring.
  - Trichogramma egg parasitoids.
  - Spray chlorantraniliprole or cartap if >10% dead hearts.

12. How do I prevent cutworm from attacking seedlings?
• Cutworms cut seedlings at soil level at night.
• Prevention: Deep plough before sowing (exposes pupae).
• Seed treatment: Imidacloprid or chlorpyrifos.
• Bran bait: Mix bran + water + insecticide (carbaryl) — apply around plants at dusk.
• Furadan (carbofuran) granules in furrow at sowing (use with care — toxic).
• Physical collar: Tin/plastic collar 3–5 cm around seedlings stem (small areas).

13. What traps can I use to monitor insect pests?
• Yellow sticky traps: Whitefly, aphids, fungus gnats, leafminers.
• Blue sticky traps: Thrips.
• Pheromone traps: Species-specific for bollworm, armyworm, fruit fly, codling moth.
• Light traps: General lepidopteran and coleopteran monitoring.
• Malaise traps: General insect diversity monitoring.
• Count and record trap catches weekly to track population trends.

14. How do I control spider mites in vegetables during hot weather?
• Spider mites thrive in hot, dry conditions.
• First action: Spray with clean water (dislodges mites and raises humidity).
• Neem oil (1–2%): Disrupts mite lifecycle.
• Abamectin: Effective miticide.
• Spiromesifen or hexythiazox: Specific miticides.
• Rotate: Never use same miticide more than twice — mites develop resistance quickly.
• Encourage predatory mites (Phytoseiidae) by avoiding broad-spectrum sprays.

15. Are there biological control options for locust?
• Green Muscle (Metarhizium acridum): Entomopathogenic fungus, slow-acting but effective.
• Neem-based products: Some effect on hoppers.
• Chemical control is generally required for large swarms.
• Biological methods are best used preventively when hoppers are young and populations are small.
• FAO EMPRES Desert Locust program coordinates international management.

16. What is integrated pest management (IPM)?
• IPM: Using multiple complementary strategies to manage pests while minimizing pesticide use.
• Components:
  1. Prevention: Resistant varieties, crop rotation, clean seed, healthy soil.
  2. Monitoring: Regular scouting, traps.
  3. Biological control: Natural enemies, entomopathogenic fungi/bacteria.
  4. Cultural control: Timing, spacing, sanitation.
  5. Chemical control: Only when ETL is exceeded, use selective pesticides.
• IPM reduces cost, resistance risk, and environmental impact.

17. How do I manage thrips in onion crops?
• Blue sticky traps for monitoring.
• Avoid dense planting; improve air circulation.
• Reflective mulch reduces thrips landing.
• Spray: Spinosad, abamectin, or imidacloprid alternating.
• Biological: Orius (pirate bug) predates thrips.
• ETL: 5–10 thrips per plant before spraying.

18. What is the best way to stop a locust swarm from destroying my crop?
• Report immediately to national plant protection organization (Locust Control Center in Pakistan: 051-9206453).
• Government aerial spraying is the primary response for large swarms.
• Individual farmers: Apply organophosphate (malathion, chlorpyrifos) spray at swarm edges.
• Noise, fires, and barriers have limited effect on large swarms.
• Prioritize high-value crops; protect nurseries and orchards.
• Stay informed via FAO Desert Locust updates.

19. How do I identify beneficial insects and protect them?
• Ladybugs: Red/orange with black spots — aphid predators.
• Lacewings: Green/brown, lacy wings — larvae prey on aphids, mites.
• Ground beetles: Dark, fast-moving — feed on soil pests.
• Parasitic wasps: Tiny, often visible near aphid colonies.
• Bees/pollinators: Essential for fruit set.
• Protect by: Avoiding broad-spectrum pesticides, planting flowers for habitat, leaving uncultivated margins.

20. How do I control root knot nematode in my vegetable field?
• Crop rotation: Avoid susceptible crops (tomato, pepper, carrot) for 2–3 years; use maize, cereals, onion.
• Resistant varieties: Use nematode-resistant rootstocks for tomato/eggplant grafting.
• Soil solarization: Cover moist soil with clear plastic for 4–6 weeks in hot season.
• Organic matter: High compost levels suppress nematodes.
• Bio-nematicides: Pasteuria penetrans, Trichoderma, neem cake.
• Chemical: Fosthiazate or oxamyl (use only when organic options insufficient).

SECTION G: CROP DISEASE MANAGEMENT

1. How do I identify yellow rust in wheat?
• Yellow (Stripe) rust (Puccinia striiformis): Bright yellow powdery pustules in stripes along the leaf.
• Appears at 10–15°C with high humidity/dew.
• Spreads rapidly in cool, moist conditions.
• Confirm: Rub pustules — orange-yellow powder comes off.
• Spreads along rows (stripes); brown rust pustules are scattered randomly.

2. What is the difference between yellow rust and brown rust in wheat?
• Yellow rust: Bright yellow, arranged in stripes along veins, cool weather (10–15°C).
• Brown rust (Leaf rust): Orange-brown, randomly scattered oval pustules, warmer weather (15–25°C).
• Both spread by airborne spores.
• Yellow rust is more aggressive and can cause greater yield loss.
• Management for both: Resistant varieties + triazole fungicide (tebuconazole, propiconazole) at first sign.

3. How do I control powdery mildew in cucurbits?
• Disease: White powdery patches on upper leaf surface.
• Favored by: Warm days, cool nights, low humidity (unlike most fungi).
• Management:
  - Remove and destroy infected leaves.
  - Spray sulfur fungicide (0.3%) or potassium bicarbonate.
  - Triadimefon, myclobutanil, or azoxystrobin.
  - Ensure proper spacing for air circulation.
  - Use resistant varieties.

4. What causes late blight in potatoes and how do I manage it?
• Caused by: Phytophthora infestans.
• Favored by: Cool temperatures (10–20°C) and >90% humidity for 2+ days.
• Symptoms: Dark water-soaked patches on leaves and stems; white mold on underside; brown rot in tubers.
• Management:
  - Use certified blight-free seed.
  - Apply mancozeb + cymoxanil preventively.
  - Avoid overhead irrigation.
  - Destroy infected plant debris.
  - Use resistant varieties (e.g., Diamant, Nicola).

5. How do I identify bacterial wilt in tomatoes?
• Sudden wilting of the entire plant without yellowing.
• Cut stem: Place in water — bacterial ooze (white threads) visible streaming out.
• Brown discoloration in stem vascular tissue.
• Caused by: Ralstonia solanacearum (soil-borne).
• No effective cure once infected.
• Prevention: Crop rotation (no solanaceous crops for 3–4 years), resistant rootstock grafting, solarization.

6. What is Karnal bunt disease in wheat and how to prevent it?
• Karnal bunt (Tilletia indica): Partial bunting of grain — black powdery mass inside grain.
• Gives fishy smell to infected grain.
• Spreads through infected seed and soil; cold, humid conditions at flowering.
• Prevention: Certified bunt-free seed; seed treatment with tebuconazole or carboxin+thiram; avoid re-using seed from infected areas.

7. How do I control smut disease in maize?
• Common smut (Ustilago maydis): Large galls on ears, leaves, stalks.
• Remove and destroy galls before they turn black and release spores.
• No fungicide is effective after infection.
• Prevention: Seed treatment with thiram or carboxin; resistant varieties.
• Crop rotation; avoid injury to plants (entry points for spores).

8. What is damping-off disease and how do I prevent it in seedlings?
• Damping-off: Seedling collapse at soil line due to Pythium or Rhizoctonia fungi.
• Favored by: Wet, cool soils, poor drainage, dense seedling beds.
• Prevention:
  - Treat seed with thiram or captan.
  - Use sterile, well-drained seedling mix.
  - Avoid overwatering.
  - Apply Trichoderma to nursery soil.
  - Drench with Ridomil (metalaxyl) if outbreak starts.

9. Why are my wheat leaves turning yellow with brown spots?
• Septoria leaf blotch: Yellow lesions with brown centers and pycnidia (black dots).
• Brown/leaf rust: Scattered brown pustules.
• Nitrogen deficiency: General yellowing from base upward (no spots).
• Zinc deficiency: Yellow-brown interveinal stripes.
• Waterlogging: Yellowing of entire lower leaves.
• Diagnosis: Check pattern, presence of spores, recent weather. Take a sample to extension officer if unsure.

10. How do I manage downy mildew in grape or cucumber?
• Symptoms: Yellow patches on upper leaf, grayish mold on underside.
• Caused by: Plasmopara viticola (grape), Pseudoperonospora cubensis (cucumber).
• Management:
  - Apply mancozeb + metalaxyl or fosetyl-Al preventively.
  - Improve air flow through pruning/spacing.
  - Avoid wetting foliage.
  - Remove and destroy infected leaves.

11. What fungicide is effective against wheat rust?
• Triazole fungicides are most effective:
  - Tebuconazole 25.9% EC @ 200 ml/acre.
  - Propiconazole 25% EC @ 200 ml/acre.
  - Cyproconazole, epoxiconazole.
• Apply at first appearance of pustules or when forecast indicates high risk.
• One spray at correct timing can prevent 50–80% yield loss.
• Do not spray within 2–3 weeks of harvest (PHI).

12. How does crop rotation help reduce soil-borne diseases?
• Most soil-borne pathogens are host-specific — without host, populations decline.
• Rotate out of susceptible crops for 2–4 years.
• Example: Wheat Karnal bunt — grow non-cereal crops for 2 years.
• Fusarium (wilt of cotton, tomato): Rotate with maize, cereals, legumes.
• Use "break crops" — crops that suppress specific pathogens.

13. What is Alternaria blight and which crops does it affect?
• Alternaria: Fungal disease causing dark brown/black spots with concentric rings.
• Affects: Tomato, potato, carrot, brassicas, sunflower, cotton.
• Favored by: Warm, humid weather, plant stress.
• Management: Mancozeb, iprodione, azoxystrobin; crop rotation; remove debris; avoid overhead irrigation.

14. How do I use resistant varieties to avoid disease losses?
• Select varieties rated Resistant (R) or Moderately Resistant (MR) in national crop catalogs.
• Resistance may break down over time (especially rusts) — stay updated on new ratings.
• Combine resistance with good crop management — resistance is not absolute.
• Consult agricultural research institute or extension officer for current recommendations.

15. What causes collar rot in my vegetable nursery?
• Collar rot: Caused by Sclerotium rolfsii or Phytophthora spp.
• White cottony mycelium at base of stem; plant collapses.
• Prevention: Trichoderma treatment of nursery mix; avoid overwatering; use raised beds; apply carboxin or Ridomil drench.

16. How do I disinfect my soil before transplanting?
• Solarization: Cover moist soil with clear plastic for 4–6 weeks in summer (kills most pathogens).
• Chemical: Formalin drench (2%) — cover for 3 days, then air for 1 week.
• Bio-fumigation: Incorporate brassica residues (glucosinolates suppress pathogens).
• Trichoderma incorporation: Mix into soil at transplanting.

17. What is the role of humidity in spreading fungal diseases?
• Most fungal pathogens require leaf wetness (>6 hours) or high humidity (>80%) for spore germination.
• High humidity promotes: Rusts, mildews, blights, botrytis.
• Manage: Improve air circulation, avoid evening irrigation, use drip instead of overhead, increase row/plant spacing.

18. How do I identify viral diseases in my crops?
• Viral disease symptoms: Mosaic patterns (light/dark green mottling), leaf curl, stunting, yellowing, necrotic streaks, ring spots.
• Spread by: Aphids, whiteflies, thrips (vector-borne); contaminated tools; seed.
• No curative treatment once infected.
• Confirmation: Send sample to plant pathology lab for ELISA or PCR test.
• Management: Control insect vectors, use virus-free seed, remove infected plants.

19. Can seed treatment prevent most seed-borne diseases?
• Yes — seed treatment is one of the most cost-effective disease management tools.
• Treats: Loose smut, covered smut, Karnal bunt, seed rots, damping-off.
• Common treatments: Thiram + carboxin (broad spectrum), tebuconazole (smuts, bunts), imidacloprid (insect + some virus vectors).
• Seed treatment does NOT protect against soil-borne or air-borne diseases that develop later.

20. How do I report a new crop disease to the agriculture department?
• Collect samples: 4–6 affected plants in paper bag (not plastic — avoids moisture buildup).
• Take clear photos of symptoms.
• Contact: District Agriculture Extension Office / Plant Protection Department.
• Pakistan: Plant Protection Department (PPD): 051-9204117.
• India: State Directorate of Agriculture / ICAR Research Station.
• USA: Contact local Cooperative Extension Service.
• Provide: Crop, variety, symptoms, area affected, recent weather, inputs used.

SECTION H: PESTICIDE USE & SAFETY

1. What is the correct dose of herbicide for wheat?
• Broadleaf weeds: Bromoxynil 225 EC @ 400 ml/acre; 2,4-D amine @ 400 ml/acre.
• Grassy weeds: Clodinafop-propargyl 15% WP @ 160 g/acre; Fenoxaprop-P-ethyl.
• Mixed weeds: Isoproturon + 2,4-D combination.
• Apply at 2-leaf to tillering stage of crop (not at heading).
• Always read label — doses vary by formulation.

2. How do I mix pesticides safely without harming myself?
• Wear full PPE before handling (see Q3).
• Work in open, well-ventilated area.
• Read label — never mix chemicals not approved for co-application.
• Add water first, then pesticide (not reverse).
• Never eat, drink, or smoke during mixing.
• In case of splash: Wash with plenty of water immediately.

3. What protective gear should I wear when spraying pesticides?
• Minimum (all spraying): Long-sleeved shirt, long trousers, closed shoes, hat.
• Recommended: Chemical-resistant gloves, safety glasses/goggles, face shield.
• For highly toxic pesticides: Full chemical-resistant coverall, respirator, rubber boots.
• Wash all clothing separately after spraying.
• Never spray without PPE — even "low-toxicity" products.

4. How do I calibrate my knapsack sprayer for accurate spraying?
• Fill sprayer with plain water.
• Walk at normal pace and spray 1 minute over a measured area (mark with pegs).
• Measure water collected in spray swath.
• Calculate: L/min ÷ (nozzle width m × walking speed m/min) = L/m².
• Adjust pressure, nozzle, or walking speed to achieve desired application rate (typically 200–250 L/acre).
• Re-calibrate when changing nozzles or operators.

5. What is the pre-harvest interval for a pesticide?
• Pre-harvest interval (PHI): Minimum days between last spray and harvest.
• Allows pesticide to break down to safe residue levels.
• Always printed on the label — check before spraying near harvest.
• Violating PHI: Illegal in most countries; makes produce unsaleable; health risk to consumers.
• Example: Chlorpyrifos on vegetables: 7–14 days PHI.

6. Can I mix two different pesticides together in one spray?
• Only if the label says "compatible" or after a jar test.
• Jar test: Mix small amounts in a glass jar; check for precipitation, separation, or gelling — if any, do not mix.
• Never mix: Alkaline pesticides + acidic ones; copper fungicides + organophosphates; most oil-based + water-based.
• Mixing benefits: Saves time; but increases poisoning risk and resistance risk.
• When in doubt: Spray separately.

7. What is a systemic pesticide vs. contact pesticide?
• Contact pesticide: Kills pests on direct contact only. Does not move through plant.
• Systemic pesticide: Absorbed by plant and distributed through vascular tissue. Kills pests feeding anywhere on plant.
• Contact examples: Pyrethrin, copper fungicides, sulfur.
• Systemic examples: Imidacloprid (insecticide), tebuconazole (fungicide), glyphosate (herbicide).
• Systemic: Better for hidden pests (stem borers, soil insects), but higher resistance risk.

8. How do I dispose of empty pesticide containers safely?
• Triple rinse: Rinse container 3 times with water, add rinsate to spray tank.
• Puncture container to prevent reuse.
• Do NOT: Burn containers, reuse for food/water storage, throw in waterways.
• Return to dealer collection point if available.
• Pakistan/India: Contact Crop Life Pakistan/India for disposal events.
• Bury punctured containers in designated waste area away from water sources.

9. What are the signs of pesticide poisoning and what should I do?
• Symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, dizziness, headache, excessive sweating, blurred vision, difficulty breathing, convulsions.
• Immediate action:
  1. Remove from exposure area.
  2. Remove contaminated clothing.
  3. Wash skin with soap and water.
  4. If eyes: Flush with clean water for 15 minutes.
  5. Call emergency services or go to hospital immediately.
• Bring the pesticide label to the hospital.
• Emergency (Pakistan): 1122 (Rescue) or hospital emergency.

10. Which pesticides are banned in Pakistan and should not be used?
• Banned in Pakistan: Aldrin, dieldrin, DDT, endrin, heptachlor, mirex, toxaphene, chlordane, endosulfan, methyl parathion, monocrotophos (restricted).
• Globally banned (Stockholm Convention POPs): 12 original + additional dirty dozen.
• Check: DRAP/NLRC registered pesticides list or visit your Agriculture Department.
• Never buy unregistered or imported pesticides from informal sources.

11. How do I store pesticides safely at home?
• Store in original labeled containers — never in food/drink containers.
• Keep in locked, cool, dry, ventilated room away from living areas.
• Store away from children, food, animal feed, seeds.
• Keep away from heat, open flames.
• Separate herbicides from insecticides and fungicides.
• Maintain inventory: Date purchased, amount remaining.

12. What is the difference between fungicide, insecticide, and herbicide?
• Fungicide: Kills or inhibits fungi and fungal diseases.
• Insecticide: Kills insects.
• Herbicide: Kills unwanted plants (weeds).
• Nematicide: Kills nematodes.
• Bactericide: Kills bacteria.
• Rodenticide: Kills rodents.
• Always use the right category for the problem — using a fungicide for insects is ineffective and wasteful.

13. How do I control broadleaf weeds in wheat without harming the crop?
• Selective herbicides: 2,4-D amine (320 ml/acre in 80L water) or bromoxynil.
• Apply at 3–5 leaf stage of wheat (not at jointing or heading).
• Spot spray: For isolated weed patches.
• Do not apply in hot conditions (>30°C) or near susceptible crops (cotton, vegetables).
• Tank mix 2,4-D + metsulfuron-methyl for broader weed control.

14. When is the right time to spray fungicide for wheat rust?
• Preventive: When conditions are favorable (cool, humid) and crop is at flag leaf to heading stage.
• Curative: At first appearance of pustules.
• Critical timing: Flag leaf (F) to ear emergence (F+2 weeks) — protects the most important yield-contributing leaves.
• Do not delay beyond 5% crop area infected.

15. What is pesticide resistance and how do I prevent it?
• Resistance: Pest population that survives pesticide that previously controlled it.
• Caused by: Overuse of same pesticide, sub-lethal doses, lack of rotation.
• Prevention:
  - Rotate pesticide groups (IRAC/FRAC/HRAC mode of action codes).
  - Use correct dose — never reduce.
  - Apply only at ETL.
  - Combine with cultural and biological controls.
  - Avoid calendar-based spraying.

16. How do I clean my sprayer after using a pesticide?
• Triple rinse: Fill with clean water, spray until empty; repeat 3 times.
• For herbicides: Add 0.5% ammonia solution for extra rinse.
• Clean nozzles, filter, and strainers separately.
• Dispose of rinsate in field (dilute) not near water.
• Store clean, dry, with nozzle removed.
• This prevents contamination of next crop — especially critical after herbicide use near sensitive crops.

17. Can overuse of insecticides harm beneficial insects like bees?
• Yes — pyrethroids, organophosphates, and neonicotinoids are highly toxic to bees and natural enemies.
• Avoid spraying during flowering when bees are active.
• Apply in early morning or evening when bees are not foraging.
• Use selective insecticides (spinosad, Bt) where possible.
• Inform neighboring beekeepers before spraying.

18. What is a bio-pesticide and how do I use neem-based products?
• Bio-pesticide: Derived from natural materials (plants, bacteria, fungi).
• Neem (Azadirachta indica): Azadirachtin disrupts insect growth and feeding.
• Neem Oil Spray: Mix 10–20 ml/L water + few drops of soap as emulsifier; spray on pests.
• Neem Seed Kernel Extract (NSKE): 50 g/L water, filter, spray.
• Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis): For caterpillars — mix as per label.
• Trichoderma: Soil fungal biocontrol — apply at 2.5 kg/acre with organic matter.

19. How many days before harvest should I stop spraying?
• Follow the PHI on each product's label — it varies by product and crop.
• General minimums:
  - Organophosphates on vegetables: 7–14 days.
  - Pyrethroids on fruit: 3–7 days.
  - Systemic insecticides: 14–21 days.
  - Some fungicides: 1–3 days.
• When in doubt, stop spraying at least 14 days before harvest.
• For export crops: Check importing country MRL requirements — stricter.

20. Where can I verify if a pesticide is registered and safe to buy?
• Pakistan: DRAP (Drug Regulatory Authority) and Department of Plant Protection (DPP), Ministry of National Food Security. Website: plantprotection.gov.pk.
• India: Central Insecticides Board & Registration Committee (CIB&RC): cibrc.nic.in.
• USA: EPA Pesticide Registration: epa.gov/pesticide-registration.
• UK/EU: EFSA and HSE Pesticide Register.
• Only buy pesticides with registration number on the label.

SECTION I: FERTILIZER MANAGEMENT

1. How much urea should I apply per acre for wheat?
• General recommendation: 1.5–2 bags (75–100 kg urea/acre) per season.
• Split application is critical:
  1. At sowing: 25–30 kg urea/acre.
  2. At crown root initiation (21 DAS): 25–30 kg urea/acre.
  3. At tillering (45 DAS): 25–30 kg urea/acre.
• Adjust based on soil test: Sandy soils need more splits; clay soils can take larger doses.

2. What is the difference between DAP and SSP fertilizer?
• DAP (Di-ammonium Phosphate): 18% N + 46% P₂O₅. High phosphorus and some nitrogen. Most widely used starter fertilizer.
• SSP (Single Superphosphate): 0% N + 16–18% P₂O₅ + 11% sulfur + 20% calcium. Slower release, contains sulfur and calcium.
• Use DAP: When only P and some N needed at sowing.
• Use SSP: When sulfur is also deficient (oilseeds, pulses, potato) or DAP is expensive.

3. How do I know if my crop has a nitrogen deficiency?
• General yellowing starting from older/lower leaves upward (nitrogen is mobile — moves from old to young leaves).
• Stunted, pale green plants.
• Premature senescence.
• In wheat: Yellowing from leaf tip, narrow leaves.
• In maize: Yellow V-shape from tip on lower leaves.
• Confirm with soil or plant tissue test.

4. What are the signs of zinc deficiency in rice?
• Khaira disease: Pale yellow-orange discoloration on leaves.
• Stunted growth, delayed tillering.
• Rusty-brown spots on lower leaves.
• Appears 2–4 weeks after transplanting.
• Management: Apply zinc sulfate 5 kg/acre as soil application or 0.5% foliar spray.

5. When should I apply phosphorus fertilizer?
• Apply at sowing/basal dose — phosphorus moves very slowly in soil.
• Cannot be applied later and reach roots efficiently.
• Mix DAP/SSP into seedbed during tillage or band-place near seed row.
• For established perennials/trees: Band or broadcast in autumn before rains.

6. What is the correct split application schedule for urea in wheat?
• Split 1: At sowing — 1/3 dose (25–30 kg/acre).
• Split 2: At crown root initiation (3 weeks after sowing) — 1/3 dose.
• Split 3: At tillering (6 weeks after sowing) — 1/3 dose.
• Never apply all urea at sowing — it volatilizes and leaches.
• In high-rainfall or irrigated conditions: 4-way split for maximum efficiency.

7. Can I mix urea and DAP fertilizer together?
• Yes, they can be mixed and applied together at sowing.
• Avoid leaving mixture exposed for >30 minutes before application — moisture absorption begins.
• Do not store the mixture — mix and apply immediately.
• Better practice: Apply DAP at seedbed preparation, urea in splits later.

8. What is a balanced fertilizer dose for maize?
• General recommendation per acre: N: 100–120 kg (equivalent to 5–6 bags urea), P₂O₅: 60–70 kg (DAP: 1.5–2 bags), K₂O: 30–40 kg (SOP/MOP: 50–65 kg).
• Apply all P and K at sowing; split N into 3 applications.
• Soil test for precise recommendation.
• Micronutrients: Apply zinc (5 kg ZnSO₄/acre) if deficient.

9. How do I apply potassium fertilizer and when is it needed?
• Apply SOP (Sulphate of Potash) or MOP (Muriate of Potash) at sowing.
• Most South Asian soils have adequate K — test first.
• K is critical for: Fruit size, disease resistance, drought tolerance, tuber quality.
• Rate: 20–40 kg K₂O/acre for most crops.
• For potatoes and vegetables: Higher rates needed.

10. What is the benefit of farm yard manure (FYM) vs chemical fertilizer?
• FYM: Improves soil structure, water-holding capacity, microbial activity, organic matter. Releases nutrients slowly. Contains all macro and micro nutrients.
• Chemical fertilizer: Precise nutrient content, fast-acting, cheaper per unit nutrient.
• Best practice: Use both — FYM as basal, chemicals as topdress.
• FYM application: 4–8 tonnes/acre per season.

11. How does compost improve soil fertility?
• Adds stable organic matter — improves soil structure, aeration, water retention.
• Slow-release of N, P, K, and micronutrients.
• Feeds soil microbes — improves nutrient cycling.
• Reduces need for chemical fertilizers over time.
• Use 2–4 tonnes/acre annually for sustained soil health.

12. What is the micronutrient deficiency most common in soils?
• South Asia (calcareous/alkaline soils): Zinc most common (>70% soils deficient).
• Globally common deficiencies: Zinc > Boron > Manganese > Iron > Molybdenum.
• In acidic soils: Phosphorus availability reduced; molybdenum deficient.
• In waterlogged soils: Iron/manganese toxicity (excess).
• Get soil tested to know your specific situation.

13. How do I apply foliar fertilizer to vegetables?
• Dissolve water-soluble fertilizer (e.g., 13-0-45, NPK 20-20-20) in water at recommended rate.
• Add wetting agent (few drops of liquid soap) for better leaf coverage.
• Spray early morning or late evening — avoids leaf burn.
• Apply when plants show deficiency or during critical growth stages.
• Common foliar: Urea (1–2%), potassium nitrate, borax (0.2%), zinc sulfate (0.3%).

14. What is the best fertilizer for a potato crop?
• High K and moderate N, P needed.
• Basal (at planting): DAP 1.5 bags + SOP 1.5 bags/acre.
• Topdress: Urea 1 bag at 30 DAS, 1 bag at 60 DAS.
• Sulfur (from SSP/SOP) improves tuber quality.
• Micronutrients: Zinc, boron if deficient.
• FYM 6–8 tonnes/acre before planting strongly recommended.

15. Is biofertilizer (Rhizobium) useful for legume crops?
• Yes — Rhizobium inoculant can fix 50–100 kg N/acre biologically, replacing 30–50% of nitrogen fertilizer need.
• Seed treatment: Mix Rhizobium culture (250 g/kg seed) with small amount of sugar/jaggery water.
• Dry seeds in shade before sowing.
• Do not mix with chemical fungicides on seed (kills bacteria).
• Also: Azotobacter (cereals), Phosphate-solubilizing bacteria (PSB) — available from agricultural departments.

16. How do I prepare compost from crop residues at home?
• Layer system: 15 cm dry carbon material (straw, stalks) + 5 cm green/wet material (leaves, kitchen waste) + thin layer of soil/FYM.
• Repeat layers to 1–1.5 m height.
• Maintain moisture: Damp but not waterlogged.
• Turn every 15–20 days.
• Ready in 2–3 months (hot summer); 4–6 months (winter).
• Add: Rock phosphate, wood ash — improves nutrient content.

17. How much boron should I apply for fruit crops?
• Soil application: Borax (10.5% B) @ 1–2 kg/acre.
• Foliar: Borax 0.2–0.3% or Solubor 0.1–0.2%, spray at flowering.
• Critical for: Pollination, fruit set, cell wall development.
• Boron deficiency: Hollow heart in apple, cork spot in pear, poor fruit set in mango.
• Note: Boron toxicity possible at high doses — soil test recommended.

18. Can I apply fertilizer through drip irrigation?
• Yes — this is called "fertigation" and is highly efficient (90%+ fertilizer use efficiency).
• Use: Fully water-soluble fertilizers only (potassium nitrate, calcium nitrate, urea, MAP, MKP).
• Do not use: SSP, DAP (insoluble), FYM.
• Inject through Venturi injector or fertilizer tank.
• Flush system after each fertigation with plain water.
• Benefits: Precise dose, directly to root zone, reduces leaching.

19. What happens if I apply too much fertilizer to my crops?
• Nitrogen over-application: Lodging, excessive vegetative growth, reduced grain quality, delayed maturity, burning of roots.
• Phosphorus excess: Locks up zinc, iron, manganese.
• Potassium excess: Reduces magnesium uptake.
• Salt damage: High fertilizer doses in dry soil can "burn" roots.
• Environmental: Nitrate leaching into groundwater; eutrophication of water bodies.
• Always follow soil test recommendations.

20. How do I interpret a soil test report to choose the right fertilizer?
• Check pH first: If acidic (<6), apply lime; if alkaline (>8), use gypsum.
• Organic matter: If <1%, add compost/FYM.
• NPK levels: Low N = apply urea; low P = apply DAP/SSP; low K = apply SOP/MOP.
• Micronutrients: Low Zn = ZnSO₄; low B = borax; low Fe = FeSO₄.
• EC: If >4 dS/m — salinity problem; focus on reclamation before fertilizing.
• Ask your extension officer to explain units and recommendations.

SECTION J: FARM MANAGEMENT & ECONOMICS

1. How do I register my farm under the Agriculture Department?

• Receive a Farm Registration Number — required for subsidies, loans, and insurance.

4. What is the Kissan Card scheme and how do I benefit?

5. How do I calculate the cost of production per acre?
• Fixed costs: Land rent, depreciation of machinery and tools.
• Variable costs: Seed, fertilizer, pesticides, irrigation, labour, transport.
• Total cost per acre = Fixed costs + Variable costs.
• Keep records of every input purchase and labour day.
• Compare with market price of produce to determine profit margin.
• Use simple notebook or free apps (AgriManager, etc.).

6. What is the minimum support price for wheat?

• India: MSP announced by Cabinet Committee on Economic Affairs (CCEA) annually.
• USA: Loan rates and marketing assistance through USDA FSA.
• Check: Respective Ministry of Agriculture or PMD website for each crop, each year.

7. How do I find a reliable buyer for my produce?
• Local mandis (wholesale markets): Best for fresh produce.
• Direct selling: To retailers, restaurants, or through Facebook/WhatsApp groups.
• Contract farming: Sign agreement with food company/exporter before sowing.
• Cooperatives: Sell through farmer collective for better bargaining.
• Online platforms: Farmdar, AgriValley (Pakistan); eNAM (India).
• Cold chain buyers: Contact food processing companies for premium prices.

8. What is the best way to store wheat grain after harvest to avoid losses?
• Dry to <12% moisture before storage.
• Clean grain: Remove chaff, weed seeds, insects.
• Metal silos: Best protection against insects, moisture, rodents.
• PICS bags (hermetic): Low-cost, effective for up to 2 years.
• Traditional kothi/bin: Seal with mud plaster; use phostoxin tablets.
• Check regularly for heating, insects, or mold.
• Avoid storing on bare ground.

9. What machinery rental services are available for small farmers?
• Government: Agriculture Mechanization Service Centers (AMSCs) in Pakistan/India offer rental.
• Private operators: Hire by the hour or acre for tractor, thresher, combine.
• Cooperatives: Shared machinery among farmer groups.

10. How do I apply for crop insurance?

11. What are the best market days and mandis for selling produce?
• Research local market schedules — most mandis have fixed weekly/bi-weekly sale days.

12. How do I form a farmers' cooperative in my village?

13. Is there a government scheme for solar tube wells for small farmers?

14. How do I export my agricultural produce?
• Register with TDAP (Trade Development Authority of Pakistan) or your national export authority.

• Ensure produce meets importing country MRL and quality standards.
• Use certified packing facilities.
• Find international buyers via TDAP trade fairs, Alibaba, or export chambers.

15. What is the difference between a Kissan card and an agricultural credit card?
• Kissan Card (input subsidy): Government-issued, provides subsidized inputs (seed, fertilizer, pesticide) at registered dealers. Not a loan — it's a subsidy.
• Agricultural Credit Card (bank-issued): A revolving credit facility for buying agricultural inputs; repaid after harvest. Issued by banks (HBL, MCB, ZTBL).

16. How do I access agriculture extension services in my district?

17. What training programs does Agriculture Department offer?

18. How do I reduce post-harvest losses in fruits and vegetables?
• Harvest at right maturity — avoid over/under-maturity.
• Use clean, non-damaging tools and containers.
• Sort and grade quickly after harvest.
• Cool produce immediately after harvest (pre-cooling).
• Use cold storage or evaporative cool chambers.
• Pack in ventilated crates; avoid bruising during transport.
• Post-harvest losses in developing countries: 25–40%; reducible to <10% with proper handling.

19. Where can I sell my organic produce at a premium price?
• Organic-certified shops, health food stores, premium supermarkets.
• Direct to consumer: Farmers' markets, online orders, CSA (Community Supported Agriculture) subscriptions.
• Hotels and restaurants: High-value buyers for certified organic produce.
• Export: EU and USA have strong demand for certified organic produce.
• Get certification from an accredited body (e.g., Skal, IMO, NASAA) first.

20. How do I transition my farm to organic certification?
• Transition period: 2–3 years of organic practices before certification (no synthetic chemicals).
• Contact certification body (national or international).
• Stop all synthetic pesticide, herbicide, and chemical fertilizer use.
• Build organic matter, use bio-pesticides, compost, crop rotation.
• Keep detailed records of all inputs and practices.
• Certification bodies in Pakistan: Skal International, IMO; India: APEDA-recognized bodies; USA: USDA NOP accredited certifiers.

SECTION K: FRUIT ORCHARDS & HORTICULTURE

1. What fruit trees are most suitable for mountain/highland valley conditions?
• Apple, pear, cherry, plum, peach, apricot, walnut, almond — all suited to temperate highlands.
• For sub-tropical foothills: Mango, guava, citrus, persimmon.
• Select chilling-requirement matched varieties for your altitude.
• High-altitude (1500–2500 m): Apple, cherry, walnut dominant.

2. How do I prune an apple tree?
• Best time: Late winter (dormancy), before bud break.
• Objectives: Open canopy for light penetration; remove dead/diseased wood; balance crop load.
• Remove: Water sprouts, crossing branches, branches going toward center.
• Target: 40–50% light interception at canopy.
• Heading cuts: Shorten long branches to lateral buds.
• Young trees: Train to central leader or open-center system for first 3–4 years.

3. When is the right time to harvest peach?
• Harvest when: Background color changes from green to yellow/orange; flesh gives slightly to thumb pressure; aroma develops.
• For local market: Harvest at 90% color development.
• For transport/export: Harvest at 75–80% development.
• Early varieties: May–June in temperate zones; late varieties: August–September.
• Test: Cut a fruit — pit easily separates from flesh at maturity.

4. How do I control codling moth in apple orchards?
• Pheromone traps: Place 3–5 per acre from bud break to monitor adult emergence.
• Spray at: 250 degree-days (base 10°C) after first moth capture.
• Insecticides: Chlorantraniliprole, spinosad, indoxacarb (rotate to prevent resistance).
• Kaolin clay: Physical deterrent on fruit surface.
• Mating disruption: Pheromone dispensers throughout orchard.
• Remove and destroy dropped fruit; clean bark for overwintering sites.

5. What is the planting distance for walnut trees?
• Standard/large orchards: 8–10 m × 8–10 m (12–15 trees/acre).
• High-density (semi-dwarf): 5–6 m × 5–6 m (25–35 trees/acre).
• Walnuts are large trees — sufficient space essential.
• Consider: Wind direction (need windbreaks), sunlight access, future canopy size.

6. How do I graft a fruit tree onto a local rootstock?
• Common methods: T-budding, chip budding (summer); whip-and-tongue, cleft grafting (dormancy).
• T-budding: Best for thin-bark plants; insert bud under T-cut bark.
• Cleft graft: Cut rootstock stem, insert 2 scions into cleft; seal with grafting wax.
• Time: Spring at start of sap flow; high success rate.
• Use: Sharp, sterilized grafting knife; seal cuts with grafting wax or tape.

7. What is the chilling requirement for apple varieties?
• Chilling requirement: Hours below 7°C (or 45°F) needed for dormancy break.
• High-chill varieties (>1000 h): Fuji, Gala, Golden Delicious — need cold winters.
• Low-chill varieties (<600 h): DORSET GOLDEN, Anna, TropicSweet — for warm areas.
• Match variety to your location's average chilling hours.
• Insufficient chilling: Delayed, uneven budbreak; poor fruit set.

8. How do I manage fire blight in pear orchards?
• Caused by: Erwinia amylovora (bacteria).
• Symptoms: Wilted, blackened blossoms and shoots ("shepherd's crook").
• Management:
  - Prune infected wood 30 cm below visible infection; sterilize tools with bleach between cuts.
  - Apply copper bactericide at bud break and flowering.
  - Avoid excessive nitrogen (promotes succulent growth).
  - Plant resistant varieties where available.

9. How long does it take for an almond tree to start bearing fruit?
• Grafted trees: 3–4 years after planting.
• Seedling trees: 5–7 years.
• Full production: Reached at 10–12 years.
• Yields: 500–1,500 kg/acre (shelled) at maturity.
• Almonds require: Low humidity, cool winters (600–800 h chilling), dry spring.

10. What fertilizer should I apply to citrus trees?
• Per mature tree per year: N: 500–700 g; P₂O₅: 300–400 g; K₂O: 400–600 g.
• Apply in 3 splits: Pre-flowering, after fruit set, before fruit sizing.
• Micronutrients: Zinc (ZnSO₄ foliar 0.5%), iron (FeSO₄ foliar), manganese, boron.
• Organic: Apply 20–30 kg compost per tree annually.
• Avoid nitrogen after July — delays fruit color development.

11. How do I increase the size of my peach fruit?
• Thinning: Remove excess fruits at marble size — leave 1 fruit per 15 cm of shoot.
• Pruning: Maintain open canopy for light penetration.
• Potassium: Apply SOP at pit hardening stage for cell division.
• Irrigation: Do not stress during Stage II (pit hardening) and Stage III (final swell).
• Boron foliar: At pink bud stage for better fruit set.

12. When should I apply dormant oil spray to my orchard?
• Apply: Late winter, just before bud break (buds swollen but not open).
• Controls: Overwintering scale insects, aphid eggs, mite eggs.
• Temperature: Apply when >4°C; do not apply if frost expected within 24 h.
• Rate: 2–4% oil in water.
• Do NOT apply when leaves are present (phytotoxic).

13. How do I protect my fruit orchard from frost damage?
• Micro-sprinklers: Run during frost — water releases latent heat, keeps temperature at 0°C.
• Wind machines/fans: Circulate warm air from above inversion layer.
• Smudge pots / heaters: Traditional method for small orchards.
• Delay bloom: Apply Promalin or kaolin to delay budbreak in early-blooming varieties.
• Windbreaks: Reduce cold air drainage from higher ground.

14. What causes fruit cracking in pomegranate?
• Irregular water supply: Long dry period followed by heavy rain or irrigation.
• High temperatures during fruit development.
• Prevention: Mulch to maintain even soil moisture; drip irrigation; avoid waterlogging.
• Foliar calcium spray at pea size and before color break.
• Varieties: Some varieties inherently crack less (select accordingly).

15. How do I store fresh apples after harvest?
• Cold storage: 0–2°C with 90–95% relative humidity — stores up to 6 months.
• Controlled atmosphere (CA): Reduces O₂ to 1–3%, increases CO₂ — extends to 9–12 months.
• Simple cool chambers (evaporative/zero-energy): Effective for 2–3 weeks.
• Wrap individual fruits in paper; use ventilated crates.
• Do not store with ethylene-producing fruits (bananas, pears) in same room.

16. What varieties of cherry grow well in high-altitude areas?
• Sweet cherry: Bing, Burlat, Stella, Summit — cold hardy, need 1000+ h chilling.
• Sour cherry: Montmorency, Morello — hardier, lower chill requirement.
• High-altitude cherries: Grown well in Gilgit-Baltistan, Kashmir, Alps, Andes, Washington State.
• Check variety chilling hours against your local winter hours.

17. How do I manage leaf curl disease in peach?
• Caused by: Taphrina deformans (fungus).
• Symptoms: Red/pink curled, thickened leaves in spring.
• Management: Apply bordeaux mixture or copper oxychloride (1%) or lime-sulfur at leaf fall (autumn) and at bud swell (before green tip stage).
• Timely single spray is very effective — no cure after infection.

18. What is the recommended spacing for a new mango orchard in hot plains?
• Standard spacing: 10 m × 10 m (40 trees/acre).
• High density: 5 m × 5 m initially (160 trees/acre), thin after 10 years.
• Ultra-high density: 3 m × 5 m with dwarf varieties and training.
• Allow for irrigation, machinery access, and future canopy growth.

19. How do I thin apple fruits for better size and quality?
• Hand thinning: Remove excess fruits at 10–15 mm size; leave 1 fruit per 15 cm.
• Chemical thinning: Carbaryl (Sevin) spray at 10 mm fruitlet size.
• Timing: Most effective at 20–40 days after full bloom.
• Target: Leave the largest, best-shaped fruit in each cluster; remove king fruit if malformed.
• Thinning increases: Fruit size, color, return bloom next year.

20. What is the benefit of windbreaks around my orchard?
• Reduces wind speed by 50–70% on leeward side (up to 10× windbreak height).
• Reduces: Physical fruit damage, wind-rub scars, flower drop, water evaporation.
• Improves: Bee pollination (pollinators prefer calm conditions), spray application efficiency.
• Trees for windbreaks: Eucalyptus, poplar, cypress (fast-growing); mulberry, casuarina.
• Plant perpendicular to prevailing wind direction.

SECTION L: LIVESTOCK & MIXED FARMING

1. How do I grow fodder crops alongside my main crops?
• Intercropping: Grow berseem, lucerne, or ryegrass in field boundaries or in rows between main crop.
• Relay cropping: Sow fodder crop in standing maize or sugarcane before harvest.
• Dedicated fodder blocks: Reserve 10–20% of farm area for cut-and-carry fodder.
• Double-cropping: Berseem (winter) → Mott grass or maize (summer) on same land.

2. What is the best fodder crop for dairy cattle in winter?
• Berseem (Trifolium alexandrinum): Most popular in South Asia — high protein (18–22%), palatable, winter annual.
• Oats: Also winter, good for hay.
• Turnip/rape: Fast-growing, high yield.
• Lucerne (Alfalfa): Perennial, very high protein — suited to drier climates.
• Mix berseem with oats/rye to reduce bloat risk.

3. How do I manage grazing so it does not damage my crops?
• Use fencing (barbed wire, electric fence, living fences) around crop fields.
• Rotational grazing on pastures — do not continuously graze same area.
• Tethering: Tie animals near fodder sources, not crops.
• Designate separate crop and grazing areas on farm.
• After harvest: Controlled crop residue grazing is beneficial for soil (manure input).

4. Can I use animal manure directly on my crops or does it need composting?
• Fresh manure: Can burn crops (high ammonia), spread pathogens, introduce weed seeds — NOT recommended for direct application.
• Composted FYM: Safe to apply; nutrients are stable and available.
• Emergency direct application: Apply 4–6 weeks before sowing and incorporate immediately.
• Poultry litter: Very high nitrogen — must be composted or aged before use.

5. How much green fodder does a dairy cow need per day?
• Minimum: 25–30 kg green fodder per adult dairy cow per day.
• Plus: 3–5 kg dry fodder (wheat straw, hay) and concentrates (2–4 kg) depending on milk production.
• High-yielding cows (>15 L/day): Need more concentrate feed.
• Water: 35–50 liters/day.
• Consult your livestock extension officer for specific ration formulation.

6. What crops can be grown to feed both livestock and generate grain income?
• Maize: Grain for income; stover/silage for cattle.
• Sorghum: Dual-purpose — grain and fodder.
• Cowpea/mung bean: Grain sold; straw for cattle.
• Barley: Grain for sale; straw for bedding/fodder.
• Chickpea/lentil: Grain and vines both usable.

7. How do I control internal parasites in sheep grazing on crop residues?
• Strategic deworming: Before and after wet season (peak transmission).
• Anthelmintics: Albendazole, ivermectin, fenbendazole — rotate to prevent resistance.
• FAMACHA: Eye color scoring to identify anemic animals needing treatment.
• Rotational grazing: Break parasite lifecycle.
• Avoid overgrazing on wet pastures.
• Consult a veterinarian for a tailored deworming program.

8. Is it beneficial to integrate beekeeping with my orchard?
• Yes — highly beneficial.
• Bees improve fruit set by 20–50% in most tree fruits.
• Apple, almond, mango, citrus all benefit greatly from bee pollination.
• Place 2–4 hives per acre during bloom.
• Avoid pesticide spraying during flowering.
• Honey and beeswax provide additional income.
• Low capital, good return investment for orchardists.

9. How do I prevent livestock from wandering into canal-irrigated fields?
• Barbed wire fencing along field and canal boundaries.
• Live hedges (eucalyptus, siris, keekar): Double as windbreaks.
• Electric fencing: Low cost, highly effective.
• Watercourse patrol: Community-appointed guards during irrigation turns.
• Village-level agreements on grazing areas.

10. What are government schemes for livestock combined with farming?
• Pakistan: Punjab Livestock Card, KPK Livestock Development Project; distributed animals and subsidized vaccines.
• India: PM Matsya Sampada Yojana, NABARD Dairy schemes, Animal Husbandry Infrastructure Development Fund.
• Global: FAO's Integrated Crop-Livestock System programs.
• Contact: District Livestock & Dairy Development Office for current schemes.
• BISP beneficiaries in Pakistan: Access to free animals through Nashonuma and livestock programs.

Punjab
Q1 Wheat | Crop Production Punjab
Q: Why is my wheat yield lower than expected despite using fertilizer?
Low yields in Punjab wheat farms are often caused by:
1. Wrong timing of fertilizer application — urea should be split into 2-3 doses, not all at once.
2. Poor seed quality — always use certified seed from AARI (Ayub Agricultural Research Institute,
Faisalabad).
3. Waterlogging from over-irrigation — reduce irrigation frequency.
4. Soil pH problems — get a soil test from the nearest Soil Fertility Research Institute (SFRI) lab before
applying fertilizers.
Source: AARI Faisalabad / Punjab Agriculture Dept
Q2 Wheat | Crop Production Punjab
Q: What is the best time to sow wheat in Punjab?
The ideal sowing window for wheat in Punjab is November 1–20. Late sowing after November 25 causes
yield loss of up to 1.5 maunds per acre per day of delay. Recommended varieties for central Punjab:
Faisalabad-2008, Galaxy-2013, Punjab-2011. For rice-wheat zones, use short-duration varieties like
NARC-2011.
Source: AARI Faisalabad
Q3 Cotton | Pest & Disease Management Punjab
Q: My cotton crop has leaf curl disease. What should I do?
Cotton Leaf Curl Virus (CLCuV) is a serious threat in Punjab. Steps to follow:
1. Remove and destroy infected plants immediately to prevent spread.
2. Control whitefly (the virus carrier) using Imidacloprid or Thiamethoxam insecticides.
3. Switch to tolerant varieties like FH-142 or CIM-600 certified by Cotton Research Institute Multan.
4. Avoid excess nitrogen fertilizer which attracts more whitefly.
Source: CRI Multan / Punjab Seed Council
Q4 Cotton | Pest & Disease Management Punjab
Q: How can I manage pink bollworm in cotton?
Pink bollworm causes 30-40% yield loss if unmanaged. Recommendations:
1. Use pheromone traps to monitor pest levels — available from Punjab Agriculture Dept offices.
2. Apply Chlorpyrifos or Emamectin Benzoate when infestation crosses economic threshold.
3. After harvest, deep plow the field to destroy overwintering pupae.
4. Do not delay harvest — timely picking significantly reduces bollworm damage.
Source: IPM Programme Punjab
Q13 Vegetables | Pest & Disease Management Punjab
Q: How can I protect my tomato crop from early blight?
Early blight (Alternaria solani) management:
1. Use disease-free certified seedlings from registered nurseries.
2. Apply Mancozeb or Copper Oxychloride fungicide at first sign of disease, repeat every 10-14 days.
3. Crop rotation — do not grow tomatoes in the same field for 2-3 consecutive years.
4. Remove and burn infected plant debris after harvest.
5. Avoid overhead irrigation — use furrow or drip irrigation to keep foliage dry.
Source: AVRDC / Punjab Horticulture Authority
Q14 General | Market Access Punjab
Q: Middlemen are taking most of my profit. How can I sell directly at better prices?
Strategies to bypass middlemen in Punjab:
1. Register with Punjab Agriculture Marketing Company (PAMCO) for direct market access.
2. Use Kissan Mandi — government direct farmer-to-buyer market linkage platform.
3. Form a Farmer Cooperative with neighbors to sell collectively to processors or exporters.
4. Use mobile apps 'Agri24' or 'Farmart Pakistan' for real-time market price information before selling.
5. Contact Agri-value chain companies like Engro Foods or Nestle Pakistan — they purchase directly from
certified farmers.
Source: PAMCO / Punjab Food Authority
Q18 General | Water Management Punjab
Q: There is no rain this season and my tube well has low water. What to do?
Drought management for Punjab farmers:
1. Switch to drought-tolerant crops: chickpea, millet (bajra), sorghum instead of water-intensive rice.
2. Use furrow or ridge irrigation instead of flood irrigation — saves 30% water.
3. Apply mulching (dry grass/straw) to reduce soil evaporation.
4. Register for Crop Insurance Scheme under Ministry of National Food Security — covers drought losses.
5. Contact ZTBL for emergency drought crop loans with relaxed collateral requirements.
Source: PCRWR / Ministry of National Food Security
Q23 Poultry | Livestock Punjab
Q: My chickens are dying suddenly in large numbers. What disease could it be?
Common causes of sudden poultry deaths in Pakistan:
1. Newcastle Disease (Ranikhet) — most common; vaccinate at 1 day, 10 days, and 28 days of age.
2. Avian Influenza (Bird Flu) — report IMMEDIATELY to nearest Livestock Dept. This is a notifiable
disease with government compensation.
3. Infectious Bursal Disease (Gumboro) — vaccinate at 14-18 days of age.
4. Aflatoxin from stored grain in feed — have feed tested for quality.
5. Visit nearest Government Veterinary Hospital — consultation and basic vaccines are provided FREE.
Source: Livestock & Dairy Development Dept Punjab
Sindh
Q5 Rice | Crop Production Sindh
Q: What fertilizer schedule should I follow for rice in Sindh?
For rice in Sindh (IRRI-6 or IRRI-9 varieties):
- DAP: 1 bag per acre at transplanting time.
- Urea in 3 splits: 1 bag at transplanting + 1 bag at tillering (25-30 days) + 1 bag at panicle initiation (55-60
days).
- Avoid excess urea — causes lodging (plants falling over).
- Zinc Sulphate (5 kg/acre): Apply if leaves show interveinal yellowing — zinc deficiency is very common in
Sindh soils.
Source: Rice Research Institute Dokri, Sindh
Q6 Rice | Pest & Disease Management Sindh
Q: My rice fields are affected by brown plant hopper. What is the solution?
Brown Plant Hopper (BPH) is critical in Sindh paddy fields. Management steps:
1. Do NOT use broad-spectrum pesticides early — they kill natural BPH enemies.
2. Use Buprofezin or Pymetrozine which specifically target BPH.
3. Drain the field for 3-4 days if infestation is heavy.
4. Grow resistant varieties: Sarshar-1 or IR-64.
5. Reduce nitrogen application — excess N increases BPH population rapidly.
Source: Rice Research Institute Dokri, Sindh
Q15 Sugarcane | Crop Production Sindh
Q: When should I apply weedicide in sugarcane and which type?
Weed management in Sindh sugarcane:
1. Pre-emergence: Apply Atrazine (1.5 kg per acre) within 2-3 days of planting while soil has moisture.
2. Post-emergence: Apply 2,4-D Amine (1 litre per acre) at 30-45 days after sprouting for broadleaf weeds.
3. Manual hoeing at 45-60 days is also effective for large fields.
4. Do not apply weedicide during hot, dry, windy weather — it will damage the crop.
Contact Sugarcane Research Institute, Thatta for local variety-specific guidance.
Source: Sugarcane Research Institute Thatta, Sindh
KPK
Q8 Maize | Crop Production KPK
Q: What is the right variety of maize for KPK conditions?
Recommended maize varieties for KPK:
- Agaiti-2002: Early maturing (85-90 days), suitable for rain-fed areas of KPK.
- Pahari Maize: Traditional variety suited to hilly and mountainous terrain.
- Azam Hybrid: Higher yield potential but needs adequate irrigation.
Sowing time: April–May for Kharif season. Contact Agriculture Research Institute Tarnab, Peshawar for
certified seed availability.
Source: ARI Tarnab, Peshawar / KPK Agriculture Dept
Q9 General | Climate Change & Disaster KPK
Q: Flash floods destroyed my crops. Where can I get help?
After flood crop damage in KPK, take these steps:
1. Report immediately to your local Union Council and Agriculture Extension Officer (Patwari).
2. Register with NDMA on helpline 1700 (toll-free) for national disaster assistance.
3. Apply for PDMA KPK relief through your district office.
4. FAO Pakistan provides crop recovery inputs (seeds, tools) — contact FAO Peshawar office.
5. ZTBL provides rescheduling of existing loans and fresh calamity loans for flood-affected farmers.
Source: NDMA / PDMA KPK / FAO Pakistan
Q10 Potato | Crop Production KPK
Q: Why are my potato tubers small and what can I do?
Small tubers are caused by several factors:
1. Late planting — plant February–March in plains; April–May in KPK highlands.
2. Poor seed quality — use certified seed from NARC Islamabad or Kaghan Development Authority.
3. Low potassium — apply SOP (Sulphate of Potash) 1 bag per acre.
4. Early harvesting — wait until tops naturally yellow before digging.
5. Compacted soil — do deep tillage (12-15 inches) before planting.
Source: NARC Islamabad / KPK Agriculture Dept
Q21 General | Pest & Disease Management KPK
Q: Pesticides are very expensive. Are there cheaper alternatives?
Low-cost pest management alternatives:
1. Neem-based spray — soak neem leaves in water overnight, filter and spray; effective against aphids
and whitefly.
2. Sticky yellow traps — coat yellow plastic sheets with motor oil; catches whitefly and aphids.
3. Trichogramma cards (biological control) — egg parasitoid cards available free from some KPK
Agriculture Dept programs.
4. Intercropping — growing maize with beans naturally reduces pest pressure.
5. Attend free IPM (Integrated Pest Management) training at Agriculture Training Institute (ATI), Peshawar.
Source: ATI Peshawar / FAO Pakistan IPM Programme

Q11 Fruits (Apple/Apricot) | Water Management Balochistan
Q: Water is very scarce in my area. How can I irrigate my orchard?
Water-saving options for Balochistan orchards:
1. Drip irrigation — reduces water use by 40-60%. Subsidized under Balochistan government schemes.
Contact BARD (Balochistan Agriculture Research & Development), Quetta.
2. Karez (Qanat) system revival — traditional underground channels being rehabilitated in many villages.
3. Rainwater harvesting ponds — traditional Sail and Khushkaba farming practices.
4. Solar-powered tube wells — AEDB (Alternative Energy Development Board) provides subsidies in
Balochistan.
Source: BARD Quetta / AEDB Pakistan
Q12 General | Extension & Education Balochistan
Q: I cannot read or write. How can I access agriculture information?
Non-literate farmers can access help through:
1. Kisan Helpline: Dial 0800-15000 (toll-free) for voice-based agriculture advice in Urdu, Pashto, and
Balochi.
2. Agriculture Extension Officer at your tehsil office — visit in person for free advice.
3. Farmer Field Schools (FFS) organized by FAO Pakistan — hands-on field demonstrations, no reading
required.
4. PTV Agriculture programs and Radio Pakistan Quetta broadcasts — daily morning farm bulletins.
Source: FAO Pakistan / Agriculture Extension Dept Balochistan
Q24 Fruits | Crop Production Balochistan
Q: My apple trees in Balochistan are not bearing fruit. What is the problem?
Apple non-bearing causes in Balochistan:
1. Pollination failure — plant at least 2 compatible varieties (e.g., Red Delicious + Golden Delicious) within
30 feet.
2. Late frost damage — use smoke pots or cover young trees with cloth during frost nights.
3. Insufficient chilling hours — some warmer areas of Balochistan need low-chill apple varieties.
4. Boron deficiency — apply Borax (200g per tree) in early spring to improve fruit set.
5. Incorrect pruning — get training from Fruit Research Station, Quetta, which also provides free grafted
saplings.
Source: Fruit Research Station Quetta / BARI Balochistan
All Provinces
Q7 General | Finance & Credit All Provinces
Q: How can I get low-cost agricultural loans in Pakistan?
Options available to farmers across Pakistan:
1. Zarai Taraqiati Bank Limited (ZTBL): Production loans from Rs. 25,000–500,000 at subsidized rates.
Apply at nearest ZTBL branch with land documents (Fard/Jamabandi) and CNIC.
2. Kissan Card: Subsidized input purchase from registered dealers — available in Punjab, piloting in
Sindh.
3. National Bank of Pakistan (NBP) Agriculture Finance: For medium to large farmers.
4. Akhuwat Foundation: Interest-free loans up to Rs. 50,000 for small farmers.
5. NRSP (National Rural Support Programme): Micro-loans and group lending in rural areas.
Source: ZTBL / State Bank of Pakistan / Akhuwat
Q16 General | Soil Management All Provinces
Q: How can I test my soil before the farming season?
Soil testing procedure across Pakistan:
1. Collect soil samples from 8-10 spots in your field, 6 inches deep. Mix together and take 500g sample.
2. Submit to nearest Soil Lab: Punjab (SFRI Lahore), Sindh (SSRI Tandojam), KPK (Soil Testing Lab
Peshawar), Balochistan (STLA Quetta).
3. Cost: approximately Rs. 200-500 per test depending on province.
4. Results show NPK levels, pH, and micronutrients.
5. The extension officer will give a fertilizer recommendation based on your specific results.
Source: SFRI / Provincial Agriculture Depts
Q17 Livestock | Livestock All Provinces
Q: My cattle are not producing enough milk. What could be the reason?
Low milk production causes in Pakistan:
1. Nutritional deficiency — feed balanced ration: green fodder (berseem/lucerne) + dry fodder +
concentrate cake.
2. Mastitis disease — check for hot, swollen udder; treat with antibiotics under veterinary guidance.
3. Irregular milking — milk at fixed times twice daily for consistent production.
4. Heat stress — provide shade and cold drinking water, especially in June-August.
5. Mineral deficiency — add mineral supplement blocks (available from Government Livestock Dept at
subsidized cost).
Source: Livestock & Dairy Development Board (LDDB)
Q19 General | Soil Management Sindh/Balochistan
Q: Soil in my field is salty and crops do not grow well. What should I do?
Soil salinity management:
1. Install sub-surface drainage — contact WAPDA's SCARP (Salinity Control and Reclamation Program).
2. Apply gypsum (calcium sulphate): 4-8 bags per acre depending on severity — available from fertilizer
dealers.
3. Grow salt-tolerant crops: kallar grass, sesbania for reclamation; SARC-1 and NIAB Saline-1 wheat
varieties for production.
4. Flood leaching — apply heavy irrigation to push salts below the root zone, then drain.
5. Contact NIAB (Nuclear Institute for Agriculture and Biology) Faisalabad for free soil reclamation advice.
Source: WAPDA SCARP / NIAB Faisalabad
Q20 General | Post-Harvest All Provinces
Q: How can I protect my stored grain from insects and avoid losses?
Grain storage pest management:
1. Clean store/kothi thoroughly before filling and spray Malathion on walls, floor and ceiling.
2. Use hermetic airtight storage bags — available through Punjab Food Dept and NGO programs.
3. Apply Aluminum Phosphide (Phostoxin) tablets: 1 tablet per 100 kg grain, seal store for 5 days.
CAUTION: Phostoxin is highly toxic — follow safety instructions strictly.
4. Maintain 12-13% grain moisture before storage — use a moisture meter.
5. Proper storage prevents 15-20% post-harvest losses per year.
Source: PASSCO / Punjab Food Dept
Q22 General | Policy & Registration All Provinces
Q: How do I register as a farmer to access government schemes?
Farmer registration in Pakistan:
1. Visit your District Agriculture Office with CNIC and land documents (Fard/Jamabandi) to get Kisan Card.
2. Register on Pakistan Agriculture Information System: pais.gov.pk
3. Punjab Kissan Card: Visit any Punjab Bank or Agriculture Dept office in your district.
4. Sindh Agri Card: Visit Sindh Agriculture Dept district office.
5. Registered farmers get access to: subsidized certified seeds, subsidized fertilizers, ZTBL loans, crop
insurance, and government input schemes.
Source: Ministry of National Food Security / Provincial Agriculture Depts
Q25 General | Climate Change & Disaster All Provinces
Q: Climate is changing and weather is very unpredictable. How do I plan my farming?
Climate-smart farming strategies for Pakistani farmers:
1. Get 10-day weather forecasts from PMD (Pakistan Meteorological Department) — download PMD app
or visit pmd.gov.pk.
2. Switch to climate-resilient crop varieties — consult PARC (Pakistan Agriculture Research Council)
Islamabad for province-specific recommendations.
3. Diversify crops — do not rely on a single crop; mix vegetables, pulses, and cereals.
4. Enroll in Crop Insurance — Livestock & Crop Insurance Scheme (LCIS) under Ministry of National Food
Security covers flood, drought, and hail damage.
5. Join a Farmer Field School — free, hands-on training in climate adaptation practices.
Source: PARC Islamabad / PMD / Ministry of National Food Security
